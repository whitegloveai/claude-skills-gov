# GASB Framework Summary for Municipal Budget Presentation

This is a concise reference for budget analysts and finance staff. It is not a substitute for the GASB Codification. Authoritative pronouncements live at gasb.org.

## Why GASB matters in budget work

The budget itself is not a GAAP document. It is a legal spending authority. But the budget structure should mirror the fund structure used in the Annual Comprehensive Financial Report (ACFR), because the ACFR presents the budget side-by-side with actuals in the budgetary comparison schedule. Consistent fund mapping between the budget book and the audited statements avoids reconciliation work for the auditor and avoids confusion for the council.

## Fund types

GASB recognizes three categories of funds: governmental, proprietary, and fiduciary. Most municipal budget narratives sit in the first two.

**Governmental funds** account for tax-supported services and are budgeted on a modified accrual basis.

- *General Fund.* The catch-all. Most departments — police, fire, parks, planning, administration — live here. Every city has exactly one general fund.
- *Special Revenue Funds.* Revenue restricted or committed for a specific purpose. Hotel occupancy tax, court technology, court security, asset forfeiture, library donations.
- *Capital Projects Funds.* Major capital acquisition and construction funded by bond proceeds, grants, or transfers from the general fund.
- *Debt Service Funds.* Resources accumulated for principal and interest on general long-term debt.
- *Permanent Funds.* Endowed funds where only earnings are spendable (uncommon for cities).

**Proprietary funds** account for business-type activities and are budgeted on a full accrual basis.

- *Enterprise Funds.* Services where fees recover most or all of the cost. Water and sewer utility, solid waste, stormwater utility, electric utility, airport, golf course.
- *Internal Service Funds.* Services provided by one department to other departments on a cost-reimbursement basis. Fleet, IT, risk management/self-insurance, central stores.

**Fiduciary funds** account for assets held in trust for others. Pension trust funds, custodial funds for property taxes collected on behalf of other entities. These are typically excluded from the budget book because the city does not control the spending.

## Budgetary basis vs GAAP basis

Most Texas cities budget on a modified cash or budgetary basis but report on a GAAP basis in the ACFR. Common reconciling items appear in the budgetary comparison schedule:

- Encumbrances treated as expenditures for budget but as commitments for GAAP
- Compensated absences accrual recognized for GAAP but not for budget
- Other postemployment benefits (OPEB) accrual recognized for GAAP but not for budget
- Capital lease principal treated as debt service for budget but capitalized for GAAP

The budget narrative should reference the budgetary basis explicitly so that readers do not try to tie the budget directly to the GAAP statements.

## Key GASB statements affecting budget presentation

- **GASB 34.** Establishes government-wide financial statements and the fund financial statements. Sets the framework for the budgetary comparison schedule that pairs the adopted budget with actual results.
- **GASB 54.** Fund balance classification: nonspendable, restricted, committed, assigned, unassigned. Budget narratives should reference the fund balance classification when discussing reserve policies and use of fund balance.
- **GASB 68 and GASB 75.** Pension and OPEB liability recognition on the government-wide statements. Budget narratives for HR and the general fund should reference contribution rates and any actuarially determined contribution shortfalls.
- **GASB 87.** Leases. Right-of-use assets and lease liabilities are now on the books. Budgets should reflect the lease payment (operating impact) rather than just the cash outflow.
- **GASB 96.** Subscription-based information technology arrangements (SBITAs). Cloud software subscriptions over a threshold are now capitalized.

## Fund balance policy and reserves

Most Texas cities adopt a fund balance reserve policy targeting 60 to 90 days of operating expenditures in unassigned general fund balance. Budgets that draw down fund balance below the policy floor should disclose the variance and the plan to restore the reserve. This is a credit rating consideration — Moody's, S&P, and Fitch all watch general fund balance trends.

## Performance measures

GASB Concepts Statement 5 encourages but does not require service efforts and accomplishments (SEA) reporting. Many cities include SEA-style performance measures in the budget book even though they are not GAAP. Performance measures categorized as input, output, and outcome give the council a useful read on what the department is doing.

- *Input.* Resources used. FTEs, dollars, equipment hours.
- *Output.* What the department produces. Permits issued, calls responded to, lane-miles overlaid.
- *Outcome.* The result of the output. Response time, citizen satisfaction, infrastructure condition score.

## Common pitfalls

- Mixing budgetary basis and GAAP basis figures in the same table without disclosure.
- Reporting fund balance without disclosing the GASB 54 classification.
- Performance measures that count only outputs and never outcomes.
- Capital outlay that does not meet the city's capitalization threshold mixed in with capitalizable items.
- Transfers between funds not disclosed as transfers (they are not revenue or expense).

## Texas-specific overlay

The Texas Comptroller's office maintains the local government financial reporting framework and the Transparency Stars program at comptroller.texas.gov. The Texas Local Government Code Ch. 102 budget timeline runs in parallel with the Tax Code Ch. 26 truth-in-taxation timeline; budgets that depend on a tax rate increase must reconcile to both.

The Texas Municipal League and the Government Finance Officers Association of Texas (GFOAT) publish updated guidance each spring; the GFOA Distinguished Budget Presentation Award sets a national standard that many Texas cities pursue.
