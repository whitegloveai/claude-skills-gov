# ALA Intellectual Freedom Framework — Working Summary

This is a working summary for library staff drafting collection development justifications and reconsideration responses. It is not a substitute for the American Library Association's full statements, which library staff should read in their entirety at least annually.

## The Three Foundational Statements

### Library Bill of Rights

The Library Bill of Rights, first adopted by the ALA Council in 1939 and last amended in 2019, sets out the library profession's commitment to a free and open society. It contains seven articles. In working summary form:

- Materials should be provided for the interest, information, and enlightenment of all people the community serves. Collection development should not be excluded because of the origin, background, or views of those contributing to the creation of the material.
- Libraries should provide materials presenting all points of view on current and historical issues. Materials should not be proscribed or removed because of partisan or doctrinal disapproval.
- Libraries should challenge censorship in the fulfillment of their responsibility to provide information and enlightenment.
- Libraries should cooperate with all persons and groups concerned with resisting abridgment of free expression and free access to ideas.
- A person's right to use a library should not be denied or abridged because of origin, age, background, or views.
- Libraries that make exhibit spaces and meeting rooms available to the public should make such facilities available on an equitable basis.
- All people have a right to privacy and confidentiality in their library use.

Article V is particularly relevant in reconsideration responses involving young adult or children's materials. A patron's age does not, by itself, justify restriction. Parents and caregivers — not the library — guide their own children's reading.

### Freedom to Read Statement

The Freedom to Read Statement, jointly produced by the ALA and the Association of American Publishers, is the field's most thorough articulation of why open access to ideas matters in a democracy. Its seven propositions, in working summary form, hold that:

1. It is in the public interest for publishers and librarians to make available the widest diversity of views and expressions, including those that are unorthodox, unpopular, or considered dangerous by the majority.
2. Publishers, librarians, and booksellers do not need to endorse every idea or presentation in the books and materials they make available.
3. It is contrary to the public interest for publishers or librarians to determine the acceptability of a book on the basis of the personal history or political affiliations of the author.
4. There is no place in society for efforts to coerce the taste of others, to confine adults to materials deemed suitable for adolescents, or to inhibit the efforts of writers to achieve artistic expression.
5. It is not the bookseller's or librarian's role to prejudge a book by the standards of any particular group.
6. It is the responsibility of publishers and librarians to give meaning to the freedom to read by providing books that enrich the quality and diversity of thought and expression.
7. Freedom to read is guaranteed by the Constitution and must be exercised if it is to be preserved.

### Freedom to View Statement

The Freedom to View Statement applies the same principles to film, video, and other audiovisual material. Its five propositions parallel the Freedom to Read Statement and extend its logic to non-print formats. It is sometimes overlooked in reconsideration responses involving DVDs, streaming media, or library-curated film programs; reconsideration policies should treat audiovisual material under the same framework as print.

## Weeding vs Reconsideration — Keep Them Separate

These are two distinct collection development activities and they should never be conflated.

**Weeding (deselection)** is a routine professional collection management activity. The library decides, on the basis of currency, condition, circulation history, relevance, and the availability of better resources, that an item no longer belongs in the active collection. The CREW method (Continuous Review, Evaluation, and Weeding) developed by the Texas State Library and Archives Commission is the standard reference. Its MUSTIE acronym — Misleading, Ugly, Superseded, Trivial, Irrelevant, Elsewhere — gives staff a working vocabulary. Weeding is not censorship. It is good stewardship of public resources.

**Reconsideration** is the formal process by which the library responds to a patron's formal challenge to material. The patron, typically using the library's adopted reconsideration form, asks the library to reconsider the material's inclusion, placement, or access. Reconsideration is a public-trust process, not a discretionary collection management decision. The response is procedural, applies the library's adopted policy step by step, and concludes with a defined appeal pathway, usually to the library board.

A weeding decision documented after a patron complaint is a reconsideration response, not a weeding decision. Calling it weeding will not change that, and treating a reconsideration as a weeding decision will fail under any serious procedural review.

## TSLAC Standards for Texas Public Libraries

The Texas State Library and Archives Commission (TSLAC) administers the accreditation standards for Texas public libraries. Accredited libraries are required to maintain a written collection development policy and a written reconsideration (challenge) policy. The TSLAC standards do not dictate the content of those policies but require that they exist, be adopted, and be followed. Reconsideration responses must follow the library's own adopted policy step by step.

## Practical Notes for the Justifier

- Map each justification factor to a specific criterion in the library's adopted collection development policy. Staff preference is not a justification.
- For acquisitions, name the professional review record (Booklist, Kirkus, Library Journal, School Library Journal, Publishers Weekly, etc., as applicable) and any awards.
- For weeding, name the CREW or MUSTIE factor that applies, and the circulation history and condition.
- For reconsideration responses, restate the patron's concern faithfully, address it directly, and never patronize. The patron has used a public-trust process and deserves a serious response.
- Intellectual freedom is sincere, not pro forma. Brief, plain, and direct beats a paragraph of slogans every time.
