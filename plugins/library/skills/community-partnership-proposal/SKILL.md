# Community Partnership Proposal

> Drafts a partnership proposal the library can send to a school, nonprofit, business, or community organization. Built for library directors and outreach coordinators who need a clean scope, a fair division of responsibilities, and the right legal pathway.

## When to Use

- "Draft the partnership proposal for the elementary summer reading program with the school district."
- "We are setting up a job-skills partnership with the workforce board — write the proposal."
- "The local credit union offered to sponsor financial literacy nights. Send back a proposal."
- "Turn this conversation with the food bank into a proposal we can both sign."
- "We are renewing the community center partnership for bilingual storytime."

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- Partner organization name and primary contact
- Proposed initiative title and one-line description
- Audience served (age bands, geographic area, populations)
- Library scope: what the library will contribute (staff time, space, books, programming, promotion)
- Partner scope: what the partner will contribute
- Term (start date, end date, renewal language if any)
- Signing authority on each side
- Whether money flows in either direction

Optional but improves output:

- Evaluation metrics (attendance, circulation lift, surveys, learning outcomes)
- Branding and recognition agreement (logo use, joint social media, signage)
- Risk and liability considerations, especially programs serving minors
- Insurance certificates if the partner will operate on city property

If partner, scope, term, or the flow of funds is missing, ask before drafting. The funds question controls whether the document is an MOU or a contract.

## Process

1. Confirm the funds question first. If money flows in either direction in exchange for goods or services, this is procurement and may fall under Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 252. Cooperative partnerships without compensation typically use an MOU.
2. Draft the Initiative Overview — one paragraph any council or board member can read and understand.
3. List Both Parties' Contributions and Commitments side by side. Imbalance is fine when it reflects reality (libraries often contribute space, programming, and books while a district contributes teachers or transportation) but must be explicit.
4. State the audience and any populations the partnership is designed to serve.
5. Define the term and renewal mechanism. Two years is a common library partnership term, often with a one-year renewal option.
6. Draft the Branding and Recognition section. Joint logos, signage, press release attribution, and social media tagging belong here.
7. Define Evaluation and Reporting. State metrics, who collects them, who owns the data, and how results are shared with both governing bodies.
8. Specify the MOU vs Contract Pathway based on the funds question.
9. Define the Approval Pathway. For a school district partnership, that usually involves the district's outside-partnerships policy and may require school board action. For a library, the city manager or library board usually approves. Signing authority should be named.

## Output Format

Plain markdown proposal. No emoji. Professional, cooperative tone — the partner will read this.

```
PARTNERSHIP PROPOSAL
[Library Name] and [Partner Organization]
[Initiative Title]
Date: [Date]

INITIATIVE OVERVIEW
[One paragraph. Plain language. Purpose, audience, and expected impact.]

BOTH PARTIES' CONTRIBUTIONS AND COMMITMENTS
Library scope:
- [Contribution]
- [Contribution]
Partner scope:
- [Contribution]
- [Contribution]

AUDIENCE SERVED
[Age bands, geographic area, populations.]

TERM AND RENEWAL
Effective: [Start date]
Through: [End date]
Renewal: [Mechanism, e.g., automatic one-year renewal unless either party
provides 60 days' notice]

BRANDING AND RECOGNITION
[Logo use, signage, press release attribution, social media practices,
joint photo and media releases for minors where applicable.]

EVALUATION AND REPORTING
[Metrics. Who collects. Who owns. Reporting cadence and audience.]

MOU vs CONTRACT PATHWAY
Recommended instrument: [MOU | Contract]
Rationale: [No funds flow / Funds flow — Ch. 252 procurement analysis
required / In-kind only]

APPROVAL PATHWAY
Library side: [City Manager / Library Board / Both]
Partner side: [Their named approval pathway]
Signing authority:
Library: [Name, Title]
Partner: [Name, Title]

ATTACHMENTS
- Photo and media release form (if programs serve minors)
- Certificate of insurance (if partner operates on city property)
- Brand standards summary (each party)
```

Length: typically two to four pages.

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**MOU vs Contract — the money question:** Where money flows in either direction for goods or services, the document may push into formal contracting under Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 252 procurement rules. Purely cooperative partnerships without compensation typically use an MOU. Exceptions apply — sole-source, interlocal agreements, and small-dollar thresholds — and the city attorney should review.

**Interlocal agreements:** Partnerships between a city library and another governmental unit (school district, county, or other municipality) may use the Interlocal Cooperation Act, Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 791.

**School district partnerships:** Most Texas school districts have a board-adopted policy on outside partnerships (often a CKC or GE-series policy in TASB Policy Online). Partnerships should align with that policy; signing authority typically rests with the superintendent or designee.

**Programs serving minors:** Partnership programs serving minors should include parental consent and photo and media release language. Background check requirements may apply for partner staff working with minors.

**Insurance and liability:** Partners operating on city property should provide a certificate of insurance naming the city as additional insured.

**Public records and retention:** Executed MOUs and contracts are public records under Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552 and retained per the TSLAC Local Government Retention Schedule.
