# End-of-Life Signals Checklist

Use these signals to support a replace-versus-repair decision in the maintenance log analysis. None of them is dispositive on its own. Two or more, occurring together, usually justify a CIP entry.

## Frequency Signals

- **Failure frequency rising** over the trailing 12 to 24 months. Plot corrective events per quarter. An upward trend that survives a moving average is real.
- **Same component failing repeatedly** within a single year (e.g., the same impeller, the same compressor, the same controller). Repeat failure of the *same* component is a stronger end-of-life signal than scattered failures of different components.
- **Shrinking mean time between failures** quarter over quarter.

## Cost Signals

- **Trailing 12-month maintenance cost approaches 60 percent of replacement cost.** Many fleet and utility programs use 60 to 80 percent as the replace threshold. Choose one and apply it consistently.
- **Major component replacement** (engine, drive, control panel) on an asset already past its rated life.
- **Cost per operating hour or cost per cycle** rising faster than inflation over a multi-year window.

## Operational Signals

- **Accumulated hours, cycles, or miles exceed the manufacturer's rated life.** Document both the rated life and the actual count.
- **Operator complaints** about reliability, safety, or usability — especially when echoed by more than one operator.
- **Downtime exceeds the unit's operational role.** A pumper truck that's out of service one week per quarter is hurting response capacity.

## Parts and Vendor Signals

- **OEM end-of-life or end-of-support announcement.** Parts will eventually disappear. Plan replacement before the announcement is two years stale.
- **Parts obsolescence.** Repair requires hunting on the secondary market or fabricating from a similar model.
- **Single-vendor service** is no longer responsive or has discontinued the service line.

## Regulatory and Safety Signals

- **Asset no longer meets the current code or design standard** that would apply to a new installation. For utility assets this often triggers a regulatory conversation (30 T.A.C. for TCEQ-regulated systems). Get the licensed engineer or operator involved.
- **Safety incident** linked to the asset's condition (a fall, a release, a near miss).
- **Cybersecurity or controls obsolescence** — an unsupported control system on a critical utility asset is an availability and integrity risk.

## How to Use in a Memo

A clean section 6 (End-of-Life Signals) names the asset, lists the two or three signals that apply, and stops. Decision recommendations belong in Section 8 (CIP) and Section 9 (PM Adjustments).
