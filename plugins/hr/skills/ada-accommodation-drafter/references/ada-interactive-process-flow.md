# ADA Interactive Process — Reference Flow

The Americans with Disabilities Act, as amended by the ADA Amendments Act (ADAAA), is codified at 42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq. Title I (employment) is implemented through regulations at 29 C.F.R. Part 1630. The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission issues enforcement guidance interpreting these provisions.

This reference summarizes the interactive process and the analytical framework. It is not legal advice. Accommodation decisions in non-routine cases should involve the city attorney.

## Key Definitions

**Disability** — a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, a record of such impairment, or being regarded as having such impairment. 42 U.S.C. § 12102. The ADAAA broadens this definition; in most cases, the threshold question is not whether the employee has a disability but whether the accommodation is reasonable and whether undue hardship applies.

**Qualified individual** — under 42 U.S.C. § 12111(8), an individual who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the employment position that such individual holds or desires. Essential functions are determined by reference to the job description, the work actually performed, and other consideration; the EEOC regulations list factors at 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(n).

**Reasonable accommodation** — modifications or adjustments that enable a qualified individual with a disability to perform essential functions or enjoy equal benefits and privileges of employment. Examples include job restructuring, modified schedules, reassignment to a vacant position, acquisition or modification of equipment, modification of examinations or training materials, provision of qualified readers or interpreters, and other similar modifications. 42 U.S.C. § 12111(9).

**Undue hardship** — significant difficulty or expense considering the nature and cost of the accommodation, the financial resources of the facility and the employer, the type of operation, and the impact on operations. 42 U.S.C. § 12111(10).

**Direct threat** — a significant risk of substantial harm to the health or safety of the individual or others that cannot be eliminated or reduced by reasonable accommodation. The analysis is individualized and based on reasonable medical judgment. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(r).

## The Interactive Process — Step by Step

### Step 1: Request Received

The request need not use magic words. Any communication from the employee (or someone on the employee's behalf, including a treating provider or family member) that the employee needs an adjustment to a workplace condition because of a medical condition starts the process. The supervisor or HR who receives the communication must treat it as an accommodation request and route it to HR for tracking.

Document the date and substance of the request immediately.

### Step 2: Identify Essential Functions

Compare the request to the essential functions of the position. The job description is the starting point, but actual practice and the supervisor's description of the work control where there is a conflict. Essential functions are the fundamental duties; marginal functions can be reassigned without changing the position.

### Step 3: Engage in Dialogue and Scope Medical Inquiry

If the disability and the need for accommodation are not obvious, the employer may request medical documentation to confirm:

- That the employee has a covered disability.
- The functional limitations relevant to the workplace.
- The expected duration of the limitations.
- The connection between the limitation and the requested accommodation.

Inquiries must be job-related and consistent with business necessity. Do not request diagnoses or full medical records. Include GINA safe-harbor language to discourage providers from disclosing genetic information.

The interactive process is iterative. The first request for documentation may not be sufficient; further dialogue may be required.

### Step 4: Identify Effective Accommodation Options

Brainstorm options that would enable performance of the essential functions. Use the EEOC's categories as a checklist: job restructuring, modified schedules or telework, equipment and assistive technology, modification of policies, reassignment to a vacant position, leave as a finite accommodation.

The employee's preferred accommodation is considered, but the employer may choose among effective accommodations.

### Step 5: Evaluate Undue Hardship

Significant difficulty or expense. The analysis considers:

- The nature and net cost of the accommodation, taking into account funding sources and tax credits.
- The overall financial resources of the facility and the city.
- The type of operation, including the composition, structure, and functions of the workforce.
- The impact of the accommodation on the operation.

Undue hardship is a fact-intensive analysis; cost alone rarely determines the outcome for a city of meaningful size. Document the analysis carefully.

### Step 6: Implement and Document

Where an accommodation is provided, document it in a written accommodation agreement. Include:

- The specific accommodation.
- The duration (defined where possible, or "subject to review").
- Review schedule (commonly 90 days, 6 months, annual).
- Employee acknowledgment and the right to request modification if the accommodation is not effective.
- Confidentiality statement.

### Step 7: Monitor and Revisit

The interactive process is ongoing. If the accommodation is not effective, if circumstances change, or if the employee's medical situation changes, the process re-engages.

## Reassignment as Accommodation

Reassignment is an accommodation of last resort when no accommodation within the current position is effective. Key points:

- The reassignment is to a vacant position for which the employee is qualified.
- The employer is not required to create a position or to displace another employee.
- In civil service jurisdictions, reassignment interacts with Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 143 (or Ch. 158) classification and seniority rules.
- Reassignment to a lower-graded position may be reasonable; the employer is not required to maintain the original pay grade unless the city's policy provides otherwise.

## Leave as Accommodation

Leave can be reasonable when it is finite, definite, and likely to enable the employee to return to the essential functions of the position. Indefinite leave is generally not reasonable. Leave as accommodation runs separately from FMLA (29 U.S.C. § 2601 et seq.) entitlement; an employee may exhaust FMLA and still be entitled to additional leave as a reasonable accommodation if the leave is finite and not an undue hardship.

## Common Mistakes

- Failing to recognize an accommodation request because it lacked legal phrasing.
- Demanding diagnoses rather than asking for limitations.
- Treating the employee's preferred accommodation as the only option, or as automatically off-limits.
- Failing to document the interactive process — the absence of a written record is itself evidence in litigation.
- Invoking undue hardship as a conclusion rather than a fact-intensive analysis.
- Confusing workers' compensation light-duty programs with ADA accommodation — they overlap but are separate legal frameworks.
- Skipping counsel review on cases involving direct threat, reassignment, or denial.
