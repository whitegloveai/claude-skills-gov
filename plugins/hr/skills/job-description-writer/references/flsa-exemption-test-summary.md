# FLSA Exemption Tests — Reference Summary

The Fair Labor Standards Act (29 U.S.C. § 201 et seq.) generally requires overtime at 1.5x the regular rate for hours worked over 40 in a workweek. Section 13(a)(1) of the FLSA exempts certain executive, administrative, professional, computer, and outside sales employees from minimum wage and overtime. The exemption rules are codified at 29 C.F.R. Part 541.

Three tests must usually be met to claim an exemption: (1) the salary basis test, (2) the salary level test, and (3) the duties test. Outside sales and certain computer employees have variations.

## Salary Basis Test

The employee must receive a predetermined fixed amount each pay period that is not reduced because of variations in the quality or quantity of work performed. With limited exceptions, the employee receives the full salary for any workweek in which work is performed regardless of days or hours.

## Salary Level Test

The employee must earn at least the threshold set by 29 C.F.R. § 541.600. The 2024 DOL rule raised the threshold; that rule has been the subject of ongoing litigation through early 2026. Cities should confirm with payroll and counsel which threshold is currently in effect for their classifications.

Highly compensated employees (HCEs) have a separate, higher threshold and a relaxed duties test.

## Duties Tests by Exemption Category

### Executive Exemption (29 C.F.R. § 541.100)

All of the following must be true:

- Primary duty is management of the enterprise or of a customarily recognized department or subdivision.
- Customarily and regularly directs the work of at least two or more other full-time employees or their equivalent.
- Has authority to hire or fire, or whose suggestions and recommendations as to hiring, firing, advancement, promotion, or other change of status are given particular weight.

Common public-sector fits: department directors, division managers, division superintendents.

### Administrative Exemption (29 C.F.R. § 541.200)

All of the following must be true:

- Primary duty is the performance of office or non-manual work directly related to the management or general business operations of the employer.
- Primary duty includes the exercise of discretion and independent judgment with respect to matters of significance.

Common public-sector fits: HR managers, finance analysts, senior accountants, planners, budget officers, communications managers, IT analysts performing systems-level work.

### Professional Exemption (29 C.F.R. § 541.300)

Two subtypes:

**Learned professional** — primary duty requires advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning customarily acquired by a prolonged course of specialized intellectual instruction. Knowledge must be predominantly intellectual in character and include the consistent exercise of discretion and judgment.

**Creative professional** — primary duty requires invention, imagination, originality, or talent in a recognized field of artistic or creative endeavor.

Common public-sector fits: city attorneys (learned), engineers (learned), licensed architects (learned), librarians with MLS (learned), CPAs (learned). Note that paralegals and accounting clerks generally do not qualify.

### Computer Employee Exemption (29 C.F.R. § 541.400)

Applies to computer systems analysts, programmers, software engineers, or similarly skilled workers whose primary duty is the application of systems analysis techniques and procedures, the design and development of computer systems or programs, or work in design, documentation, or modification of programs related to operating systems. Help-desk staff and computer operators generally do not qualify.

### Outside Sales Exemption (29 C.F.R. § 541.500)

Primary duty is making sales or obtaining orders, and the employee is customarily and regularly engaged away from the employer's place of business. Rarely applicable in local government.

## Analytical Questions Before Claiming Exempt Status

1. Does the position satisfy the current salary basis and salary level thresholds?
2. What is the primary duty (the principal, main, major, or most important duty)?
3. Which 29 C.F.R. Part 541 exemption category fits, if any?
4. For executive: does the role supervise two or more FTE and have meaningful input on personnel decisions?
5. For administrative: is the work directly related to management or general business operations, and does it require the regular exercise of discretion and independent judgment on matters of significance?
6. For professional: is the advanced knowledge acquired through a prolonged course of specialized intellectual instruction, not just on-the-job training or experience?
7. Have you documented the analysis in the position file so it can be defended in a DOL audit or a wage-and-hour lawsuit?

## Public-Sector Notes

- Section 7(o) of the FLSA permits public agencies to provide compensatory time in lieu of overtime under specific conditions, including a pre-existing agreement.
- Volunteers under 29 U.S.C. § 203(e)(4) are not employees if they meet the statutory criteria.
- Section 7(k) provides partial overtime exemptions for fire protection and law enforcement personnel based on a work-period basis.
- Public safety on-call time, training time, and shift change activities have specific FLSA treatment that should be addressed in the position's pay rules, not in the JD.

## Common Mistakes

- Calling a position "exempt" because it is salaried. Salary alone is insufficient.
- Treating "supervisor" as automatically exempt without the duties analysis.
- Failing to update the FLSA analysis when duties change.
- Bundling marginal management tasks into a job to claim the executive exemption.
- Misapplying the administrative exemption to clerical or production work.

When in doubt, classify as non-exempt. The cost of paying overtime is far lower than the cost of a misclassification claim with back wages, liquidated damages, and attorney's fees.
