# Example: Remote Work Policy Compliance Review — City of Cedar Ridge

**Input the HR Director provided to the skill:**

- Draft policy text: "Cedar Ridge Remote Work Policy" — allows up to three days per week of remote work for eligible non-public-safety positions, with supervisor approval. Sets expectations for availability during "core hours" of 9:00 a.m. to 3:00 p.m. Requires employees to provide their own internet and home workspace. Permits supervisor to "monitor work product and activity as needed." Silent on FLSA timekeeping, ADA accommodation, and workers' compensation.
- Jurisdiction: City of Cedar Ridge, TX. Home-rule city, ~32,000 population. Civil service applies to police and fire (Ch. 143); no Ch. 174 collective bargaining. The policy excludes sworn personnel.

# MEMORANDUM

**TO:** Daniel Ahn, City Manager
**FROM:** Dr. Karen Asher, HR Director
**DATE:** May 10, 2026
**RE:** Compliance Review — Draft Remote Work Policy

## I. ISSUE

This memo reviews the draft Cedar Ridge Remote Work Policy against federal and Texas employment law. The policy permits up to three days per week of remote work for non-public-safety positions with supervisor approval. The review focuses on FLSA wage-and-hour treatment, ADA accommodation interplay, Texas workers' compensation coverage, and workplace monitoring limits.

## II. SUMMARY OF FINDINGS

- **High:** No FLSA timekeeping protocol for non-exempt remote workers.
- **High:** Policy treats remote work as a discretionary benefit and is silent on ADA reasonable accommodation requests for remote work.
- **Medium:** Workers' compensation coverage at the home worksite is not addressed; Tex. Lab. Code Title 5 still applies and the home becomes a worksite during work hours.
- **Medium:** "Core hours" requirement, while operationally reasonable, may conflict with accommodation requests for medical appointments or caregiver responsibilities.
- **Low:** "Monitor work product and activity as needed" language is overbroad and creates avoidable expectation-of-privacy litigation risk; tighten to specific, disclosed monitoring methods.
- **Low:** Equipment and home internet provisions raise potential Texas Payday Law issues if costs effectively reduce pay below applicable thresholds.

## III. DETAILED FINDINGS

### Finding 1 — FLSA Timekeeping for Non-Exempt Remote Workers

**Risk Level:** High
**Controlling Authority:** 29 U.S.C. § 207; 29 C.F.R. Part 785 (hours worked); 29 C.F.R. Part 516 (records).

**Discussion:** Non-exempt remote employees are entitled to overtime for hours worked over 40 in a workweek. The City must capture all hours worked, including off-the-clock work such as checking email at night or starting before the workday officially begins. The draft policy is silent on timekeeping and on "no off-the-clock work" expectations. DOL Field Assistance Bulletin 2020-5 puts employers on notice that they must use reasonable diligence to capture remote hours and pay for them.

**Recommendation:** Add a section titled "Timekeeping for Non-Exempt Employees" requiring:

> Non-exempt employees working remotely must record all hours worked in the City's timekeeping system, including time worked before, after, or in addition to scheduled hours. Employees may not perform work off the clock. Overtime requires prior supervisor approval; however, all reported hours over 40 in a workweek will be paid at the overtime rate regardless of pre-approval. Supervisors who become aware of unreported work hours must direct the employee to report them.

### Finding 2 — ADA Reasonable Accommodation Interplay

**Risk Level:** High
**Controlling Authority:** 42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq.; 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o); EEOC technical assistance on telework as reasonable accommodation.

**Discussion:** The draft frames remote work as a manager-approved benefit. The ADA may require remote work as a reasonable accommodation for a qualified individual with a disability when remote work would enable the employee to perform the essential functions of the position without undue hardship. A blanket discretionary framing without a parallel accommodation pathway can be used as evidence of failure to engage in the interactive process.

**Recommendation:** Add a paragraph stating:

> Nothing in this policy limits an employee's right to request remote work as a reasonable accommodation under the Americans with Disabilities Act. Accommodation requests are handled separately by Human Resources under the City's ADA Reasonable Accommodation Policy and are evaluated on the position's essential functions and an individualized interactive process.

### Finding 3 — Workers' Compensation Coverage at the Home Worksite

**Risk Level:** Medium
**Controlling Authority:** Tex. Lab. Code Title 5 (workers' compensation); Tex. Lab. Code § 401.011(12) (course and scope).

**Discussion:** Texas workers' compensation covers injuries arising out of and in the course and scope of employment. A home worksite during scheduled work hours is generally within course and scope, but the home introduces ambiguous fact patterns (e.g., a fall while moving between a home office and a personal bathroom). The policy should provide guidance to employees and supervisors on reporting and on what constitutes the designated workspace, without disclaiming coverage the City cannot lawfully disclaim.

**Recommendation:** Add:

> Employees working from an approved home worksite remain covered by the City's workers' compensation program for injuries arising out of and in the course and scope of employment. Employees must designate the primary remote workspace on their Remote Work Agreement and must report any work-related injury immediately to their supervisor and Human Resources, consistent with the City's Workers' Compensation Reporting Procedure.

### Finding 4 — Core Hours and Accommodation/Scheduling Conflicts

**Risk Level:** Medium
**Controlling Authority:** 42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq. (ADA); 29 U.S.C. § 2601 et seq. (FMLA, including intermittent leave); 38 U.S.C. § 4316 (USERRA leave for service obligations).

**Discussion:** A rigid 9:00 a.m. to 3:00 p.m. core-hours rule is operationally reasonable but should not foreclose individualized accommodation. Intermittent FMLA, medical appointments tied to an ADA accommodation, and USERRA-protected military duties may require schedule flexibility within or outside core hours.

**Recommendation:** Add a sentence: "Core hours may be modified for documented leave or accommodation under FMLA, ADA, USERRA, or other applicable laws."

### Finding 5 — Monitoring Language

**Risk Level:** Low
**Controlling Authority:** Public-employee Fourth Amendment expectations under *O'Connor v. Ortega*; Texas common-law intrusion-upon-seclusion principles; the city's IT acceptable use policy.

**Discussion:** "Monitor work product and activity as needed" is unbounded. Specific, disclosed monitoring (timekeeping system entries, ticket system metadata, work product review, MDM on city-issued devices) reduces expectation-of-privacy claims and aligns with the City's existing IT acceptable use policy.

**Recommendation:** Replace the open-ended sentence with a list of specific monitoring methods, all of which the city already deploys: ERP timekeeping, MDM on city-issued laptops, work product review by supervisor, and ticket-system metadata. State that personal devices are not monitored.

### Finding 6 — Equipment and Home Internet

**Risk Level:** Low
**Controlling Authority:** Tex. Lab. Code Ch. 61 (Texas Payday Law).

**Discussion:** Texas does not require employers to reimburse home-internet costs by statute, but the City should be consistent. The draft requires the employee to "provide their own internet and home workspace" without addressing whether the City furnishes equipment. Make the equipment provision explicit.

**Recommendation:** Specify that the City provides the laptop, monitor, and required peripherals; that the employee is responsible for a reliable internet connection meeting a minimum specification; and that no payroll deductions will be made for home-office costs.

## IV. OPEN QUESTIONS FOR LEGAL COUNSEL

1. Should the City require a written Remote Work Agreement signed by the employee as a precondition to approval, and what is the appropriate retention schedule?
2. Are there any open OSHA or general-duty-clause exposures at the home worksite the City should address through self-inspection certifications?
3. Does the City want to address tax considerations for employees who may relocate outside Texas while working remotely (state withholding, nexus)?
4. Should police and fire personnel covered by Ch. 143 civil service be addressed under a separate policy rather than carved out by exclusion?

## V. IMPLEMENTATION CONSIDERATIONS

- **Approval:** City Manager approval is sufficient; the policy does not require council action under the City's existing personnel rules.
- **Training:** Supervisor briefing on FLSA timekeeping for remote non-exempt staff. HR-led session, 45 minutes, before effective date.
- **Communication:** Employee-facing summary distributed at least 14 days before the effective date.
- **Effective date:** Recommend the start of the next pay period after adoption.
- **Forms:** Develop a Remote Work Agreement form and a one-page home-office self-certification.
