# HANDBOOK UPDATE PACKAGE — Section 4.2, Exempt and Non-Exempt Classification

**Triggering Authority:** 29 C.F.R. Part 541; DOL final rule at 89 Fed. Reg. 32842 (Apr. 26, 2024); status of rule and applicable threshold confirmed by City Attorney as of May 2026.
**Effective Date:** July 1, 2026
**Approval Path:** City Manager (delegated personnel policy authority); Council briefing at June regular meeting.
**Prepared By:** Dr. Karen Asher, HR Director
**Date:** May 10, 2026

## PART 1 — REASON FOR UPDATE

The U.S. Department of Labor issued a final rule on April 26, 2024 amending 29 C.F.R. Part 541 to raise the salary threshold for the executive, administrative, professional, and computer exemptions under the Fair Labor Standards Act. The rule has been subject to litigation; as of the date of this memo, the City Attorney has confirmed the threshold currently controlling Cedar Ridge's classifications and has recommended that the City update its handbook language to be principle-based rather than threshold-specific, so that the handbook does not require re-amendment each time a court or agency action changes the dollar figure.

Cedar Ridge has identified eight positions presently classified exempt whose salaries are below or close to the current threshold. HR and the Finance Department have conducted FLSA exemption reviews of those positions. Three positions will be reclassified to non-exempt effective July 1, 2026; the other five will receive salary adjustments to remain exempt. The handbook update brings Section 4.2 into alignment with current FLSA practice and provides employees with the information they need about timekeeping and overtime if their classification changes.

## PART 2 — REDLINED SECTION

**Section 4.2 — Exempt and Non-Exempt Classification**

The City classifies each position as exempt or non-exempt under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) (29 U.S.C. § 207) and the implementing regulations at 29 C.F.R. Part 541. ~~Exempt status requires a minimum annual salary of $35,568 and that the position meet the executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales duties test under 29 C.F.R. § 541.100 et seq.~~ **Exempt status requires that the position satisfy the salary basis test, the salary level test set by the U.S. Department of Labor at 29 C.F.R. § 541.600 as in effect from time to time, and the duties test for the applicable exemption category under 29 C.F.R. § 541.100 et seq. The Human Resources Department maintains the FLSA exemption analysis for each position.**

Non-exempt employees are entitled to overtime at one and one-half times their regular rate for hours worked over 40 in a workweek, in accordance with FLSA § 7 (29 U.S.C. § 207). ~~Compensatory time may be granted in lieu of overtime in accordance with City policy.~~ **Public-sector employees may receive compensatory time in lieu of overtime in accordance with FLSA § 7(o) (29 U.S.C. § 207(o)) and the City's Compensatory Time Policy. Sworn fire and police personnel are subject to the work-period overtime calculation under FLSA § 7(k) (29 U.S.C. § 207(k)) and the City's adopted work periods.**

**[New paragraph]** **The City reviews FLSA classifications annually and whenever a position's duties or salary changes materially. A position's classification is determined by HR in consultation with the Department Head and, where appropriate, the City Attorney. Employees who are reclassified will receive written notice of the effective date, the timekeeping expectations, and the overtime and compensatory time rules that will apply.**

## PART 3 — PLAIN LANGUAGE SUMMARY

The City has updated the way it explains FLSA exempt and non-exempt classifications in the handbook. The substance is unchanged: exempt means salaried under federal rules with no overtime; non-exempt means hourly under federal rules with overtime at time-and-a-half for hours over 40 in a workweek.

The change makes two things clearer. First, FLSA salary thresholds are set by the U.S. Department of Labor and they sometimes change. The handbook now references the applicable threshold rather than printing a specific dollar amount. Second, the City reviews classifications annually and whenever a job changes materially. If your classification changes, HR will tell you in writing, explain the timekeeping and overtime rules that will apply, and walk through the change with your supervisor.

If you have been notified that your classification is changing on July 1, 2026, your supervisor and HR will meet with you individually before that date. If your classification is not changing, this update does not affect you.

Questions: Human Resources, x4100 or hr@cedarridge.tx.gov.

## PART 4 — SUPERVISOR TALKING POINTS

- The handbook now describes FLSA classification in principle-based language rather than printing a specific salary threshold.
- The substance of exempt and non-exempt is unchanged. Exempt is salaried with no overtime; non-exempt is hourly with overtime over 40 in a workweek.
- The City reviews FLSA classifications annually.
- Three positions are being reclassified to non-exempt effective July 1, 2026. Affected employees have been notified individually.
- Newly non-exempt employees will use the timekeeping system to record all hours worked. They cannot work off the clock. Overtime requires pre-approval, but all worked hours over 40 will be paid as overtime.
- Compensatory time may be granted in lieu of overtime under FLSA § 7(o) and the City's policy.
- Sworn fire and police personnel are unaffected by this update; they operate under the § 7(k) work-period rules.

**FAQ 1: "Is my pay going down?"** Answer: No. Reclassifications are designed to be revenue-neutral on base pay. Some employees will see overtime when they work it; some will see compensatory time accruals. No employee's base pay is being cut.

**FAQ 2: "Why do I have to track my hours now?"** Answer: The FLSA requires non-exempt employers to record all hours worked. The City's timekeeping system already supports this; HR will provide a 30-minute training before July 1.

**FAQ 3: "Can I keep working off the clock if I want to?"** Answer: No. Off-the-clock work is a wage-and-hour violation that creates exposure for the City and for you. If you are working, you must record it.
