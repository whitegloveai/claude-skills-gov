# Employee Handbook Updater

> Drafts a handbook update package — redline, plain-language summary, supervisor talking points, and council resolution language where needed. Built for HR directors who have to update a 200-page handbook one section at a time and never have time to communicate the change well.

## When to Use

- "DOL issued a new salary threshold rule. Update our FLSA classification language."
- "Council adopted a paid parental leave benefit last night. Update the leave section."
- "The Fifth Circuit decision on bathroom policies came down. Walk our handbook through it."
- "We're moving to a new PTO accrual structure effective Jan 1. Draft the redline."
- "Texas legislature passed a new posted-notice requirement. Update the workplace notices section."
- An auditor or counsel flagged stale language and a written update is required for the personnel file system.

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- The handbook section being updated, in its current form
- The triggering change: statute, regulation, court decision, council action, or audit finding (with citation)
- Target effective date
- Whether council adoption is required or whether the city manager can adopt under delegated authority

Optional:

- The communication plan (all-hands meeting, supervisor cascade, employee email, intranet posting)
- The CBA, if Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 174 applies
- Any related policies that may need conforming edits
- Translation requirements (Spanish, other) if the city has adopted bilingual personnel materials

If the user has not provided the current section text or the triggering authority, ask before drafting. A redline without the source is fiction.

## Process

1. Confirm and cite the triggering authority precisely — case and court, statute and section, C.F.R. cite, or resolution and adoption date.
2. Identify every affected paragraph. Flag conforming edits needed in adjacent sections (definitions, leave interactions, pay practices).
3. Draft the redline. Use strikethrough for removed language and bold or bracketed notation for additions. Preserve the handbook's existing structure.
4. Draft the Plain Language Summary in two to four short paragraphs: what changed, when, what the employee must do, and who to contact. Avoid jargon.
5. Draft five to ten Talking Points for supervisors, including the likely employee questions and the right answer to each.
6. If council action is required (benefits, compensation plan, or civil service changes), draft a one-page resolution reciting triggering authority, action, and effective date.
7. Build the Implementation Timeline backward from the effective date: HR draft, counsel review, council adoption if needed, communication, supervisor training, effective date, and intranet update.
8. Note related forms or acknowledgments to revise at the same time.

## Output Format

Plain markdown package with four parts. Length: 800 to 1,400 words. Tone: precise on the redline, plain on the summary.

```
HANDBOOK UPDATE PACKAGE — [Section Name]

Triggering Authority: [Citation]
Effective Date: [Date]
Approval Path: [Council / City Manager]
Prepared By: [Author]
Date: [Date]

PART 1 — REASON FOR UPDATE
[2–3 paragraphs explaining what triggered the change and why the
current language is insufficient.]

PART 2 — REDLINED SECTION

[Original paragraph 2, with edits marked.]

[2–4 short paragraphs.]
```

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**EEOC AI guidance:** If the handbook update touches on AI-assisted hiring, evaluation, screening, or monitoring, the analysis should flag the EEOC's 2023 technical assistance on AI in employment decisions. Disparate-impact testing and accommodation pathways should be addressed in the policy language itself, not left to operational practice.

**Federal triggers commonly engaged:** FLSA (29 U.S.C. § 207) and 29 C.F.R. Part 541; ADA/ADAAA (42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq.); FMLA (29 U.S.C. § 2601 et seq.); Title VII (42 U.S.C. § 2000e et seq.) and PWFA; USERRA (38 U.S.C. § 4301 et seq.).

**Texas authorities:** Tex. Lab. Code Ch. 21 (TCHRA); Tex. Lab. Code Ch. 61 (Payday Law); Tex. Lab. Code Title 5 (workers' comp); Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 614; Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 143; Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 174 (where adopted).

**Public records:** Adopted handbooks are public under Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552. Drafts may be exempt under § 552.111; counsel-reviewed redlines under § 552.107. Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552 (Public Information Act) is separate from Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code Ch. 552 (TRAIGA).

**Retention:** Per Texas State Library Local Government Retention Schedule GR. Maintain superseded versions with effective and superseded dates.

**Acknowledgments and at-will:** Employees sign a new acknowledgment when the handbook updates. Texas is an at-will state — the handbook should not create an implied contract, and the acknowledgment should restate the at-will relationship.
