# Common Contract Risk Categories — Municipal Reference

Risk taxonomy for redline review of city contracts. Each category lists the typical red-flag triggers, the city-favorable position, and the Texas-law constraints that govern.

## 1. Indemnification

**City-favorable:** Vendor indemnifies, defends, and holds harmless the city, its officials, officers, employees, and agents from third-party claims arising from vendor's negligence, willful misconduct, or breach. No mutual indemnity from the city.

**Red flags:**
- Vendor narrows scope to "gross negligence" only
- Vendor adds a floor or threshold below which indemnity does not apply
- Vendor caps indemnity at contract value or insurance limits
- Vendor demands mutual indemnification from city
- Vendor excludes IP infringement indemnity

**Texas constraint:** A Texas city generally cannot indemnify a third party beyond what the legislature has authorized. Indemnification clauses obligating the city to indemnify the vendor are problematic and may be void as exceeding statutory authority. See Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Ch. 130 (anti-indemnity in construction) where applicable.

## 2. Limitation of Liability

**City-favorable:** No cap, or cap not less than the greater of contract value and insurance limits, with carve-outs for indemnification obligations, confidentiality breaches, IP infringement, gross negligence, and willful misconduct.

**Red flags:**
- Cap at fees paid in the prior 12 months (common SaaS default — usually too low)
- Cap that swallows the indemnification carve-out
- Exclusion of consequential, indirect, or punitive damages with no carve-outs
- Cap measured by an amount lower than mandatory insurance limits

**Practical note:** A cap below the insurance floor creates a coverage gap.

## 3. Insurance

**City-favorable:** Vendor maintains general liability, auto liability, workers' compensation, professional liability (for services), and cyber/data breach (for IT and SaaS) at limits stated in the contract; city named as additional insured (except for workers' comp and professional liability); waiver of subrogation; primary and non-contributory coverage; 30-day notice of cancellation.

**Red flags:**
- Vendor strikes additional-insured status
- Vendor strikes primary/non-contributory language
- Cyber limits insufficient for the data volume at risk
- Self-insured retentions vendor cannot demonstrate

## 4. IP Assignment and Data Ownership

**City-favorable for IT/SaaS:** City owns its data, can export at any time and on termination, vendor has only a limited license to use city data to provide the services. Deliverables specifically developed for the city are work-for-hire owned by the city.

**Red flags:**
- Vendor claims a perpetual license to city data for "service improvement"
- Vendor claims aggregated or "de-identified" data without geographic or purpose limits
- Termination data export gated on payment of an additional fee
- Deliverables licensed rather than assigned

## 5. Termination

**City-favorable:** Termination for convenience on 30 days' notice with payment only for work performed; termination for cause without cure period for material breach; termination for non-appropriation (essential for multi-year contracts).

**Red flags:**
- Vendor strikes termination for convenience
- Vendor demands early termination fee
- Vendor strikes non-appropriation termination
- Cure period too long (over 30 days for material breach)

**Texas constraint:** Multi-year contracts that bind future councils on appropriations are problematic. The non-appropriation clause is generally required to keep the contract from being an unconstitutional debt under Tex. Const. Art. XI § 7.

## 6. Dispute Resolution — Arbitration, Venue, Governing Law

**City-favorable:** Texas law governs; venue lies exclusively in the district court of the city's home county; no mandatory binding arbitration.

**Red flags:**
- Mandatory binding arbitration in a non-Texas forum
- Governing law of another state
- Venue in vendor's home county or state

**Texas constraint:** Mandatory arbitration provisions in some public contracts are subject to specific statutory restrictions. Public construction contracts have rules under Tex. Loc. Gov't Code § 271.154 and Tex. Gov't Code § 2009.005 regarding mediation and arbitration. The city attorney should verify any arbitration clause against the current statute and case law before agreeing.

## 7. Change Orders and Pricing

**City-favorable:** Fixed pricing for the term; change orders only by written amendment signed by an authorized representative; no automatic price escalation beyond a stated index cap.

**Red flags:**
- Vendor reserves unilateral price increases
- Auto-renewal at then-current pricing
- Verbal change orders allowed

## 8. Payment Terms

**City-favorable:** Net 30 after acceptance and proper invoice; no late fees beyond Texas Prompt Payment Act allowances under Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 2251; no automatic ACH debit.

**Red flags:**
- Net 10 or pre-payment
- Late fees exceeding Tex. Gov't Code § 2251.025 interest
- Pre-authorization of ACH debits

## 9. Performance and Payment Bonds

For construction over $50,000, vendor furnishes statutory payment bond; over $100,000, performance bond. See Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 2253.

**Red flags:**
- Vendor proposes letter of credit or other security in lieu of statutory bond on a project subject to Ch. 2253

## 10. Public Records Cooperation

**City-favorable:** Vendor cooperates with the city's TPIA obligations; vendor confidential markings are not determinative — only Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552 exceptions control; vendor will provide responsive records held on its systems within the city's response window.

**Red flags:**
- Vendor demands the city assert § 552.110 trade secret on its behalf without § 552.305 process
- Vendor refuses to deliver records held in its systems
- Vendor charges a fee for TPIA cooperation

## 11. Audit Rights

**City-favorable:** Three-year audit window after final payment; vendor maintains records per generally accepted accounting principles; reasonable access to books and records and the right to take copies.

**Red flags:**
- Audit window under one year
- Vendor restricts auditor identity or charges for audit support
- No audit right for federally funded contracts (where federal rules require audit retention)

## 12. Force Majeure

**City-favorable:** Mutual; defined narrowly to genuinely uncontrollable events; pandemic risk explicitly addressed post-2020; obligation to mitigate; suspension only, not excuse of payment obligations for work already performed.

**Red flags:**
- One-sided in vendor's favor
- "Economic hardship" or "increased costs" listed as force majeure
- Force majeure as a basis for indefinite suspension without termination right

## 13. Assignment

**City-favorable:** Vendor may not assign without city's written consent; city may assign to a successor governmental entity or in connection with a transfer of governmental function.

**Red flags:**
- Vendor reserves right to assign to affiliates without notice (common in private equity ownership churn)

## 14. Confidentiality

**City-favorable:** Vendor protects city's confidential information; city's confidentiality obligation is qualified by the TPIA — the city cannot agree to absolute confidentiality on records that are public.

**Red flags:**
- Mutual confidentiality with no TPIA carve-out
- Confidentiality term outlasts contract by more than 3–5 years
- Liquidated damages for breach

> **Advisory scaffolding — not legal advice. Review by city attorney required.**
