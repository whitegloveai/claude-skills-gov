# Example — Short-Term Rental Ordinance Impact Analysis

> Council Member Reyes submitted a draft short-term rental ordinance (Ord. No. 2026-014) for legal review before introduction at the June 9, 2026 council meeting. City Attorney Patricia Mendoza directed the assistant city attorney to run an impact analysis.

---

```
MEMORANDUM — Ordinance Impact Analysis
Privileged work product

To:        Patricia Mendoza, City Attorney
           Council Member Yolanda Reyes
           Daniel Ahn, City Manager
From:      Assistant City Attorney
Date:      May 12, 2026
Re:        Proposed Ordinance No. 2026-014 — Short-Term Rental
           Registration and Regulation
           Sponsor: Council Member Reyes (District 3)

1. ORDINANCE SUMMARY

Ord. No. 2026-014 establishes a registration program for short-term
rentals (defined as residential properties offered for occupancy for
less than 30 consecutive days) within the City of Cedar Ridge. The
ordinance:

(a) Requires annual registration with the Planning Department, with
    a $250 application fee.
(b) Imposes a 4 percent gross receipts hotel occupancy tax.
(c) Caps occupancy at two persons per bedroom plus two additional
    occupants.
(d) Requires a local responsible party available 24/7 within 30
    miles.
(e) Prohibits short-term rental of any dwelling that is not the
    operator's homestead (no non-owner-occupied STRs).
(f) Prohibits any advertising of a short-term rental that has not
    been issued a registration certificate, regardless of where
    or to whom advertised.
(g) Authorizes administrative penalty of up to $500 per day per
    violation and revocation of registration for repeat violations.

2. AUTHORITY FOR ADOPTION

Cedar Ridge is a home-rule city under Tex. Const. Art. XI § 5 and
Tex. Loc. Gov't Code § 5.004. Under § 51.072 the city has the full
power of self-government not denied by statute. Hotel occupancy tax
is authorized under Tex. Tax Code Ch. 351, which permits cities to
impose HOT on short-term rentals (Tax Code § 351.0025). Registration
and operational regulation are within general police-power authority
to protect health, safety, and welfare.

3. CHARTER CONFLICTS

None identified. Charter Art. III § 3.04 reserves regulatory ordinance
authority to the council; this ordinance proceeds by standard
ordinance procedure. Charter Art. VII § 7.02 reserves tax-rate
adoption to the council; the HOT rate is set by ordinance.

4. EXISTING CODE CONFLICTS

- Code Ch. 18 (Hotel Occupancy Tax) already imposes 7 percent HOT on
  hotels and motels. The proposed 4 percent STR rate must be
  reconciled. Recommend amending Ch. 18 to consolidate, not parallel
  schemes.
- Code Ch. 28 (Zoning) lists permitted uses by district; "short-term
  rental" is not currently defined. Recommend Ch. 28 amendment to
  add definition and the zoning-district-by-district overlay.
- Code Ch. 8 (Nuisance) Art. III noise standards govern; ordinance
  references them by cross-reference and should be conformed.

5. STATE PREEMPTION ANALYSIS

Provision (a) — Registration: No express preemption. Within police
power.

Provision (b) — 4 percent HOT: Authorized by Tex. Tax Code § 351.0025
and § 351.003.

Provision (c) — Occupancy cap (two per bedroom + two): Comparable
provisions have been challenged. In *City of Austin v. Zaatari*, 615
S.W.3d 172 (Tex. App. — Austin 2019), the court struck portions of
an Austin STR ordinance on substantive due process and equal
protection grounds; occupancy limits were not the principal basis
of that decision but the case bears on aggressive STR regulation
generally. The proposed cap should withstand challenge but is
contestable.

Provision (d) — Local responsible party: Within police power.
Sustainable.

Provision (e) — Homestead-only prohibition: HIGH RISK. The provision
struck in *Zaatari* was nearly identical — Austin's ban on
non-homestead STRs in residential zones. The Austin court of appeals
held the ban violated substantive due process and exceeded the city's
police-power authority. Whether subsequent litigation has changed
the analysis should be verified, but on current authority a
homestead-only rule is unlikely to survive challenge. The HB 2127
preemption regime (Property Code subject area) also reaches this
provision. RECOMMEND STRIKING (e).

Provision (f) — Advertising prohibition for unregistered properties:
Reaches the Business & Commerce Code (advertising regulation) and
implicates HB 2127 preemption. Also raises First Amendment concerns
(see Section 8).

Provision (g) — Administrative penalty up to $500/day: Within Tex.
Loc. Gov't Code § 54.001 ceiling for health-and-safety ordinances.

6. FEDERAL PREEMPTION ANALYSIS

None directly. Fair Housing Act considerations apply if the ordinance
is applied discriminatorily but no facial issue. ADA accessibility
requirements may apply to the registration process for applicants
with disabilities.

7. ENFORCEMENT MECHANISM ANALYSIS

Provision (g) authorizes administrative penalty and revocation.
Process requirements:

- Pre-deprivation notice and opportunity to be heard before
  revocation. The draft is silent. RECOMMEND amendment to specify a
  notice-and-hearing process under Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 214
  Subch. C or comparable due process scaffolding.
- Standing to enforce: Code Compliance Division; appeal to municipal
  court or hearing officer.
- Aggregate penalty cap and statute-of-limitations considerations.
  Recommend cap on per-incident accrual.

8. CONSTITUTIONAL CONCERNS

First Amendment — Advertising prohibition (Provision f): Reaches
truthful commercial speech (an offer to rent a residential dwelling).
Under *Central Hudson Gas v. Public Serv. Comm'n*, 447 U.S. 557
(1980), the regulation of non-misleading commercial speech requires
a substantial governmental interest, direct advancement of that
interest, and narrow tailoring. Banning advertising of
unregistered-but-otherwise-lawful properties is broad. RECOMMEND
narrowing to "advertising that misrepresents the registered status"
or limiting to advertising on the city's own platforms.

Equal Protection — Homestead-only rule (Provision e): Creates a
classification between resident and non-resident property owners.
Sustained under rational-basis review but bears on the Zaatari
analysis. See Provision (e) recommendation.

Takings — Substantive due process / regulatory takings: Strict
prohibitions on a previously lawful use may give rise to takings
claims, particularly for owners who acquired property in reliance
on the prior regulatory regime. Recommend grandfathering or
amortization provision.

Fourth Amendment — Registration process does not authorize entry.
If future amendments authorize inspection, build in administrative
warrant procedure.

9. POLICY OBSERVATIONS

- The City of Caddo Falls (neighboring jurisdiction) adopted a
  comparable registration ordinance in 2024 without the homestead-
  only rule and reports manageable enforcement.
- HOT collection mechanics work best when STR platforms collect and
  remit. Recommend coordination with major platforms during
  implementation.
- Local responsible party requirement should accommodate professional
  property managers.

10. RECOMMENDATION

Defer pending revisions. Specifically:

(i)   Strike Provision (e) (homestead-only ban). Substantial
      likelihood of successful Zaatari-style challenge.
(ii)  Narrow Provision (f) (advertising) to misrepresentation only.
      First Amendment concerns.
(iii) Add due process scaffolding to Provision (g) (revocation
      hearing procedure).
(iv)  Add grandfathering or amortization provision.
(v)   Reconcile HOT rate with Code Ch. 18.
(vi)  Add zoning definition and overlay through Code Ch. 28
      amendment, on a parallel track.

With those amendments the ordinance is defensible. As drafted, it
is not.

11. ATTACHMENTS

Redline of Provisions (e), (f), and (g) with suggested amendments.

End of memo.
```
