# State Preemption Quick Reference — Texas Municipal Ordinances

A categorized list of major Texas state-law preemption regimes affecting city ordinances. This is a screening tool, not an exhaustive treatise. Verify the current statute and case law before relying on any conclusion.

## 1. The Texas Regulatory Consistency Act — HB 2127 (2023)

Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 1, Subch. C-1 (added by HB 2127, 88th Reg. Sess.).

Broad-field preemption preventing cities from adopting or enforcing ordinances in eight subject-matter codes:

- Agriculture Code
- Business & Commerce Code
- Finance Code
- Insurance Code
- Labor Code
- Natural Resources Code
- Occupations Code
- Property Code

**Test:** Has the legislature occupied the field by enacting any statute in the listed codes that "regulates" the activity? If yes, a city ordinance "regulating" the same activity is preempted.

**Litigation status:** Constitutionality has been challenged on home-rule grounds. Subsequent legislation (including the GROW Texas / Texas Regulatory Consistency Act amendments) has continued to expand the regime. Verify current case law before relying on preemption or non-preemption.

**Common applications:** employer paid-leave mandates; predictable-scheduling ordinances; tenant-protection ordinances exceeding state law; tree-preservation regulations on private property; aspects of short-term rental regulation that touch the Property Code.

## 2. Firearms and Knives

- Tex. Loc. Gov't Code § 229.001 — firearms preemption. Cities cannot regulate firearms, ammunition, or knife transport, ownership, possession, or transportation beyond narrow listed exceptions (employee policy, public buildings under specific rules, public parks per statute).
- Tex. Loc. Gov't Code § 229.002 — extends preemption to firearm component parts and accessories.

## 3. Oil, Gas, and Energy

- Tex. Nat. Res. Code § 81.0523 (HB 40, 2015) — oil and gas operations preemption. Cities cannot regulate "an oil and gas operation" except commercially reasonable surface regulation. Bans on hydraulic fracturing are preempted.
- Tex. Util. Code § 39 — retail electric service is preempted.
- Tex. Loc. Gov't Code § 250.008 — energy source preemption. Cities cannot restrict utility, residential, commercial, or industrial energy sources or limit appliance fuel choice.

## 4. Plastic and Container Regulation

- Tex. Health & Safety Code § 361.0961 — preemption of ordinances regulating containers and packaging (bag bans were struck down on this basis in *City of Laredo v. Laredo Merchants Ass'n*, 550 S.W.3d 586 (Tex. 2018)).

## 5. Telecommunications and Right-of-Way

- 47 U.S.C. § 253 — federal preemption of regulations that "prohibit or have the effect of prohibiting" telecom service.
- Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 283 — state telecom right-of-way regime.
- Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 284 — small wireless facilities in right-of-way. Caps on application fees and required timeline.

## 6. Cable Television

- Tex. Util. Code Ch. 66 — state-issued cable franchise; cities cannot impose additional franchise requirements.

## 7. Housing and Tenant Regulation

- Tex. Prop. Code Title 8 occupies much of the residential landlord-tenant field.
- HB 2127 sweeps in Property Code as a preempted subject area.
- Tex. Loc. Gov't Code § 250.007 — preemption of source-of-income discrimination ordinances in housing rental.
- Federal Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. § 3601 et seq.) — additional limits on housing classifications.

## 8. Short-Term Rental Regulation

No statute expressly preempts all STR regulation. However:
- HB 2127 reaches Property Code provisions that may bear on STR.
- HB 14 / SB 929 efforts in successive sessions have considered express STR preemption — verify current statute.
- *City of Austin v. Zaatari*, 615 S.W.3d 172 (Tex. App. — Austin 2019) struck portions of an STR ordinance on substantive due process and equal protection grounds.
- A typical defensible STR ordinance regulates noise, occupancy, parking, and safety — all traditional police-power topics — without banning the use outright in residential zones.

## 9. Building Codes and Construction

- Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 214 — building codes adopted by cities.
- Tex. Loc. Gov't Code §§ 214.211–214.218 — limits on cities' authority to require certain residential building products and methods.
- Tex. Loc. Gov't Code § 250.005 — limits on regulating residential aesthetics in single-family neighborhoods.

## 10. Sign Codes

- *Reed v. Town of Gilbert*, 576 U.S. 155 (2015) — content-based sign regulation is subject to strict scrutiny.
- Tex. Transp. Code Ch. 391, 393, 394 — state-controlled signs along highways and rights-of-way.
- HB 2127 may sweep in commercial speech sign regulation through the Business & Commerce Code.

## 11. Tobacco, Alcohol, and Cannabis

- Tex. Health & Safety Code Ch. 161 — state tobacco regulation, with local authority to enact stricter rules in some areas.
- Tex. Alcoholic Beverage Code — alcohol regulation largely preempted with limited local-option authority.
- Cannabis: federal Schedule I; Texas Compassionate Use Program limited; cities cannot legalize but may decline local prosecution priority — verify with current state legislation and case law on local prosecutorial discretion.

## 12. Immigration

- Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 752, § 752.053 (SB 4, 2017) — prohibits local "sanctuary" policies; mandates cooperation with federal immigration authorities. Sustained in *City of El Cenizo v. Texas*, 890 F.3d 164 (5th Cir. 2018).
- Federal preemption of state and local immigration regulation under *Arizona v. United States*, 567 U.S. 387 (2012).

## 13. Drones and Aviation

- 49 U.S.C. § 41713 — federal preemption of regulation of aircraft operations.
- Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 423 — drone regulation; preempts most city restrictions.

## 14. Vacation, Sick Leave, and Paid Leave Mandates

- HB 2127 (Labor Code) — preempts paid-leave mandates on private employers.
- *Texas Ass'n of Bus. v. City of Austin*, 565 S.W.3d 425 (Tex. App. — Austin 2018) — Austin paid-sick-leave ordinance preempted by Minimum Wage Act before HB 2127.

## 15. Anti-Discrimination Beyond State Statute

Cities have adopted anti-discrimination ordinances reaching classifications beyond Tex. Lab. Code Ch. 21. HB 2127 (Labor Code) and subsequent legislation have narrowed authority. Constitutionality remains contested. Verify current state of law.

## 16. Election Administration

- Tex. Elec. Code occupies the field of election administration. Local discretion is narrow and statute-prescribed.
