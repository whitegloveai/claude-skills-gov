# Texas Local Government Incentive Authorities — Quick Reference

A working summary of the statutory toolkit Texas cities, counties, and economic development corporations use to structure incentives. Not legal advice. Always confirm with city or county attorney before commitment.

## Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 380 — Municipal Economic Development Loans and Grants

**Who it covers:** Home-rule and general-law municipalities.

**What it authorizes:** A city may establish and provide for the administration of one or more programs to promote state or local economic development and to stimulate business and commercial activity in the municipality. Programs may include loans and grants of public money, the use of city personnel, and the use of city services and facilities, at no charge or below cost.

**Common structures:**

- Property tax rebate (rebate of city-collected ad valorem tax, structured as a grant tied to incremental valuation)
- Sales tax rebate (rebate of municipal portion of sales tax, often tied to retail or destination projects)
- Cash grant on milestones (capex documented, jobs reached, certificate of occupancy)
- Fee waivers (impact fees, building permit fees, plan review fees)
- Infrastructure cost-share (street, water, wastewater extensions to serve the project)

**What it cannot fund:** Operations of an existing business with no public benefit nexus; pure relocation incentives that violate the gift clause if no return benefit is structured; activities outside the legitimate public purpose of economic development.

**Adopted in open meeting. Agreement is a public record.**

## Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 381 — County Development and Diversification

**Who it covers:** Counties.

**What it authorizes:** A commissioners court may develop and administer a program for state or local economic development and may make loans and grants of public money. Substantively parallels Ch. 380 for the county level.

**Common structures:** Property tax rebate of the county levy, infrastructure cost-share on county roads, cash grants tied to capex and job milestones.

**Counties cannot abate sales tax they do not levy.** A county cannot rebate a municipal sales tax.

## Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 504 — Type A Economic Development Corporations

**Funding source:** A dedicated local sales and use tax (typically a quarter-cent or half-cent) approved by voters.

**Eligible uses (industrial focus):** Land, buildings, equipment, facilities, expenditures, improvements, and infrastructure for projects related to manufacturing, industrial, research and development, military facilities, recycling, distribution centers, small warehouse facilities, and primary job creation. Job training facilities and related infrastructure are eligible.

**Not eligible for Type A:** Most quality-of-life uses, retail, and entertainment unless tied to a primary employer.

**Governance:** A board of directors appointed by the city council. Operates as a Texas nonprofit corporation. Acts of the board are subject to council oversight; certain expenditures require council approval.

## Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 505 — Type B Economic Development Corporations

**Funding source:** Same sales tax mechanism as Type A.

**Eligible uses:** Everything a Type A may fund, plus a broader quality-of-life set: parks and recreation facilities, sports venues, affordable housing, public safety facilities, water and sewer infrastructure, professional and amateur sports facilities, tourism and convention facilities, learning centers, transportation facilities, and certain affordable housing.

**§ 505.158 public hearing:** Certain Type B projects (notably those not in the core categories listed in § 505.151) require a separately noticed public hearing before the corporation may undertake the project. Read the section carefully — the hearing requirement is project-specific and triggers based on category.

**Annual reporting:** § 505.404 requires Type B corporations to file an annual report with the city. § 504.351 does the same for Type A.

**Governance:** Board appointed by city council. Many decisions also require council approval under § 505.159 for Type B projects.

## Tex. Tax Code Ch. 311 — Tax Increment Reinvestment Zones (TIRZ)

**What it does:** Designates a defined geographic zone in which property tax increment above a baseline year is captured into a fund used to pay for public improvements within the zone. Cities and counties may participate; school districts may participate but the state offsets school participation.

**Use cases:** Urban core redevelopment, brownfields, district-scale infrastructure, mixed-use redevelopment that requires public improvements.

**Process:** Creation requires a preliminary project plan, a public hearing, an ordinance designating the zone, and a TIRZ board.

## Tex. Tax Code Ch. 312 — Property Tax Abatement

**What it does:** Authorizes a city or county to abate (forgive) up to 100% of incremental property tax for up to 10 years on real property and personal property in a designated reinvestment zone.

**Prerequisites:**

- The taxing unit must have adopted abatement guidelines and criteria within the prior two years.
- The property must be in a reinvestment zone designated by the taxing unit.
- A separately executed abatement agreement between the taxing unit and the property owner.

**Stacking:** Ch. 312 abatement may be combined with Ch. 380/381 grants, but the combined incentive must remain a defensible public purpose use. Watch for gift-clause concerns when the combined incentive nears or exceeds the projected public benefit.

## Common Drafting Pitfalls

- Treating Ch. 380 as a blanket authority without articulating the public benefit nexus.
- Failing to cite § 505.158 when a Type B project triggers a separate hearing.
- Stacking abatement and grant programs without modeling the combined recapture if performance fails.
- Drafting clawback provisions that recapture less than the disbursement, leaving the city net-negative on a failed deal.
- Treating site selector confidentiality as absolute; § 552.131 confidentiality is narrow and time-limited.
