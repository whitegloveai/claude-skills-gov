# Proclamation Writer

> Drafts a city proclamation in standard whereas form for the mayor's or council's signature. Built for city secretaries assembling weekly proclamation packets and for council aides handling recognition requests.

## When to Use

- "Draft a proclamation declaring April as Fair Housing Month."
- "The mayor wants to recognize the high school robotics team at next week's meeting. Write the proclamation."
- "We have a retirement proclamation request from public works."
- "Draft a proclamation for National Police Week."
- "The county is asking for a proclamation in support of mental health awareness — draft it for the mayor's signature."
- Any recognition or awareness item where the council or mayor will issue a written proclamation for presentation.

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- Occasion: recognition month or week, awareness day, anniversary, retirement, organizational milestone, individual recognition, or community partner recognition
- Key facts establishing significance: dates, numbers, history, accomplishments
- Signing authority: mayor, mayor pro tem, council as a body
- Presentation date and venue
- Recipient (individual, organization, or general public)

Optional:

- City-specific connection: how does this occasion touch the city, its staff, its residents, or its history
- Quotations or statistics the requester wants reflected
- Photograph or seal placeholder
- Spanish or other language version if the recipient or audience warrants

If the occasion is generic and the requester has not provided a city-specific connection, ask. The strongest proclamations link a national or state observance to local people and places.

## Process

1. Identify the proclamation type: observance (month/week/day), individual recognition, organizational recognition, anniversary, or retirement.
2. Confirm signing authority and presentation date. A proclamation read aloud at a meeting may include a "presented this [date]" line; one mailed or framed off-meeting omits it.
3. Draft the heading block: city name, office of the mayor (or office of the council), proclamation title.
4. Draft 4 to 6 whereas clauses, one fact each: history of the observance, scope or statistics, local connection, alignment with city values, contributions of the recipient, invitation to residents. One fact per clause reads better aloud than stacked claims.
5. Draft the "now, therefore" operative paragraph: "Now, therefore, I, [Name], Mayor of the City of [Name], Texas, do hereby proclaim [period] in the City of [Name] and call upon all residents to [participate / observe / recognize]." For a council-issued proclamation: "Now, therefore, be it proclaimed by the City Council of the City of [Name] that..."
6. Add the signature block: mayor's signature line (and city secretary attestation when the council is the issuing body), issuance date, and a seal placeholder.
7. Keep total length between 250 and 400 words. A proclamation should read aloud in under two minutes.
8. Use neutral, civically appropriate language. A proclamation is a public act; do not editorialize, take a partisan position, or make claims that cannot be substantiated.

## Output Format

A single-page proclamation formatted in standard whereas form, on the city's office of the mayor letterhead placeholder.

```
CITY OF [NAME]
OFFICE OF THE MAYOR
PROCLAMATION

[TITLE OF THE PROCLAMATION, in all capitals]

WHEREAS, [first fact establishing the observance or recognition, with
specific dates and references]; and

WHEREAS, [second fact, often the scope or national context]; and

WHEREAS, [third fact, the local connection to the City of [Name] and
its residents]; and

WHEREAS, [fourth fact, alignment with the city's mission, values, or
policy commitments]; and

WHEREAS, [fifth fact, the contributions of the individual or
organization being recognized, or the city's call to action]; and

WHEREAS, [optional sixth fact, the invitation to residents];

NOW, THEREFORE, I, [Name], Mayor of the City of [Name], Texas, do
hereby proclaim [the period or recognition] in the City of [Name],
Texas, and call upon all residents [to participate, to observe, to
recognize, etc.].

PROCLAIMED this [day] of [month], [year].

____________________________________
[Mayor's Name]
Mayor, City of [Name], Texas

[ATTEST, if the council is the issuing body:]

____________________________________
[City Secretary Name]
City Secretary

[SEAL PLACEHOLDER]
```

Tone: formal, dignified, neutral. The proclamation is read aloud in a public meeting; cadence matters. Avoid bureaucratic phrasing, lists of statistics that read poorly aloud, and any language that takes a contested political position.

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**Public record status:** Proclamations issued by the mayor or council are public records under Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552 and are open to inspection without exception. The drafting file is generally a public record once the proclamation is presented.

**Retention:** Proclamations retain permanently under TSLAC Schedule GR, Item 1000-32 (Annual reports and statistical summaries; recognition and proclamation records are commonly filed under the same permanent category) or under the city's analogous local item. Many city secretaries maintain a permanent proclamation book in the office of the city secretary.

**Establishment Clause and viewpoint neutrality:** Proclamations of explicitly religious observances should be drafted with care to avoid Establishment Clause concerns. A proclamation that recognizes a religious community's contribution to the city is generally appropriate; a proclamation that endorses a religious doctrine is not. The city attorney should review any proclamation touching religion, gender identity, race, or partisan politics before signature.

**No statutory authority required:** A mayor or council may issue a proclamation under the general powers of the office. The document has no legal effect; it is a public act of recognition.

**Verify before signing:** Spelling of recipient names and titles, accuracy of statistics, presentation date, and that the city attorney has cleared any sensitive content.
