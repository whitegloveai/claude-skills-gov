# Program Flyer Drafter

> Drafts the words on a parks and recreation program flyer. Built for recreation supervisors and program coordinators who need clean, accessible copy ready for the city's graphic designer or the print shop.

## When to Use

- "Draft the flyer copy for the youth summer baseball league."
- "We're launching a senior pickleball clinic. I need a flyer."
- "Adult yoga at the community center, eight weeks starting in September — write the flyer."
- "Family movie night at the city park, October 14. Draft a flyer."
- "We need a flyer for the after-school art program."
- Any program-promotion piece distributed publicly that needs consistent accessibility, registration, and contact language.

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- Program name
- Audience (youth, adult, senior, family, all-ages) and any age cutoffs
- Dates and times (full date range, day-of-week pattern, start/end times)
- Location (facility name and street address)
- Fee structure (resident vs. non-resident if applicable, sibling discounts, scholarship availability)
- Registration deadline and mechanism (online portal URL, walk-up at community center, mail-in)
- Capacity limits and any waitlist policy
- Instructor or coordinator name and contact (phone and email)

Optional:

- Equipment or attire participants must bring
- Accessibility considerations (sensory-friendly sessions, accessible route to the facility, sign language interpreter availability on request)
- Language access needs (Spanish translation, other languages prevalent in the jurisdiction)
- Sponsor or partner credit lines
- Photo release language for programs that will be photographed for city marketing

If audience age range, location, fee, or registration mechanism is missing, ask before drafting. Do not invent prices or capacity.

## Process

1. Confirm the audience and age range. Programs serving minors need a clear cutoff and a parent or guardian signature line on the registration form.
2. Confirm fees and whether scholarships exist. If the city offers them, the flyer should say so plainly.
3. Draft a headline under 60 characters. Sentence case unless city style says otherwise. No marketing superlatives.
4. Write a one- or two-sentence hook naming the activity and the audience. Hook is the only place where some warmth is allowed.
5. Build the Key Details Block as a scannable Who / When / Where / Cost grid.
6. Write the description paragraph in plain language at roughly an 8th-grade reading level. Describe what participants will actually do.
7. Draft the Accessibility Statement using the city's standard ADA reasonable-modifications language. Name the contact and give a request deadline (typically 72 hours before the program start).
8. Draft Registration Instructions: URL or location, deadline, payment methods, refund policy in one line.
9. Close with the program contact and city website.
10. If the jurisdiction has a significant non-English population, flag the need for a parallel translated version.

## Output Format

Plain markdown copy block, ready for a designer to lay out. No emoji. No images.

```
[HEADLINE — under 60 characters]

[Hook — one or two sentences naming the activity and the audience.]

WHO: [Age range, any prerequisites]
WHEN: [Date range, days, times]
WHERE: [Facility name, street address, city]
COST: [Resident fee] / [Non-resident fee] / [Scholarship line if applicable]

[Description paragraph — 60 to 100 words. What participants will do, what
they will learn or gain, what to bring, instructor credentials if relevant.]

ACCESSIBILITY
The City of [City] does not discriminate on the basis of disability. The
City will provide reasonable modifications to programs and services for
participants with disabilities consistent with Title II of the Americans
with Disabilities Act, 42 U.S.C. § 12131 et seq. To request a
modification, an auxiliary aid, sign language interpreter, or large-print
materials, contact [Name] at [phone] or [email] at least 72 hours before
the program start. The City of [City] also complies with applicable state
disability-rights provisions and welcomes participants of all abilities.

REGISTRATION
Register online at [URL] or in person at [Community Center address] by
[deadline]. Payment by [methods]. Refunds available [policy in one line].

QUESTIONS
[Coordinator name], [title]
[phone] | [email]
City of [City] Parks and Recreation | [city website]
```

Length: 200 to 350 words of body copy plus the labeled blocks. Aim for a flyer that fits one side of an 8.5-by-11.

Tone: clear, warm but not sappy, no buzzwords. The flyer should sound like the city wrote it, not a marketing agency. Avoid "fun-filled," "exciting opportunity," and any phrasing that talks down to the reader.

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**ADA Title II (42 U.S.C. § 12131 et seq.):** Cities are public entities under Title II, not Title III. The functional posture for parks programs delivered to the public, however, closely mirrors Title III public-accommodations practice (42 U.S.C. § 12181 et seq.), and many cities reference both. The flyer must include a reasonable-modifications statement and a contact for accommodation requests. The 72-hour notice convention is a planning aid, not a statutory minimum; cities cannot refuse a reasonable modification solely because the request arrived late.

**Language access:** Where 5% or more of the population is limited English proficient, federal LEP guidance (DOJ Title VI of the Civil Rights Act) supports producing translated copy. The practical expectation in many Texas communities is bilingual program materials.

**Photo release:** If the program will be photographed for city marketing, the registration form should carry the release language, not the flyer.

**Public records:** The final flyer is a public record under Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552. Drafts may be exempt under § 552.111 until distributed. Promotional materials retain per the Texas State Library Local Government Retention Schedule.
