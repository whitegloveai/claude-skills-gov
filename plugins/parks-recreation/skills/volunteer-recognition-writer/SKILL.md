# Volunteer Recognition Writer

> Drafts genuine, specific volunteer recognition in three formats: letter, social post, and spoken remarks. Built for parks directors and program staff who want to honor a volunteer in a way that actually sounds like the city, not a marketing template.

## When to Use

- "Draft a recognition letter for our 200-hour volunteer at the community center."
- "We're honoring three volunteers at the next parks board meeting. I need 3 minutes of remarks."
- "Write a social post recognizing our trail-cleanup crew from Saturday."
- "Our after-school homework club coordinator is retiring. Draft a letter from the Director."
- "National Volunteer Week is next month. Recognize our top 10 volunteers."
- Any moment the city is publicly thanking a volunteer or group in writing or in person.

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- Volunteer name (or group name) and pronouns if known
- Role or program supported
- Hours contributed and the time period over which they were given
- Specific contributions — what the volunteer actually did, not just the program name
- Specific impact — a number, a story, a moment where the work mattered
- Signing or speaking authority (Parks Director, Mayor, Parks Board Chair, City Manager)
- Format needed: letter, social post, spoken remarks, or all three

Optional:

- A direct quote from a staff member, program participant, or fellow volunteer about the recognized person
- The setting where remarks will be delivered (parks board meeting, council meeting, end-of-year recognition event)
- Any award title or formal honor being conferred
- Photo permission status (whether the volunteer has consented to being photographed and named publicly)

If hours, specific contributions, or specific impact are missing, ask before drafting. Generic praise reads as filler and undermines the recognition.

## Process

1. Confirm photo and naming permission. Some volunteers prefer not to be named publicly. The letter format is fine without consent; social posts and spoken remarks require it.
2. Identify the single most specific thing the volunteer did. This becomes the anchor of every format.
3. Identify the single most specific outcome. A number, a story, a participant's name (with consent).
4. For a letter: open with the named volunteer, name the specific contribution by the end of paragraph one, develop the impact in paragraph two, close with a personal note from the signing authority.
5. For a social post: 60 to 120 words. One photo placeholder. Lead with the contribution, not the hours.
6. For spoken remarks: 350 to 500 words, roughly 2 to 3 minutes at conversational pace. Open with the volunteer's name, tell one short story, name the impact, conclude with the formal recognition language.
7. Avoid generic praise. "A true asset to our community" is filler. Name the work.
8. Use the volunteer's preferred name and pronouns. If unknown, confirm before drafting.

## Output Format

Three sub-formats. The skill produces whichever the user requests; if "all three" is requested, all three blocks are produced in one document.

**Letter format (1 page, on city letterhead):**

```
[Date]

[Volunteer Name]
[Address]

Dear [Name],

[Opening — name the volunteer and the specific contribution by the end
of this paragraph. Not "thank you for your service" — name the work.]

[Impact paragraph — the specific outcome, a number or a story. Two to
four sentences.]

[Personal close — one or two sentences from the signing authority. May
include a hand-written postscript line for the signer to add personally.]

Sincerely,

[Name]
[Title]
City of [City]
```

**Social post format (60–120 words):**

```
[Volunteer name and role in 1 sentence — lead with the contribution]

[2–3 sentences naming the specific work and the specific impact]

[Closing — invitation to volunteer, or recognition of the program]

[Hashtags optional, no more than 3]

[Photo: name the subject of the photo and confirm permission]
```

**Spoken remarks format (350–500 words, 2–3 minutes):**

```
[Opening line addressed to the body — "Members of the Parks Board" or
"Mayor and Council"]

[Sentence naming the volunteer and the recognition.]

[One short story — a specific moment. 4 to 6 sentences.]

[Impact paragraph — the number, the outcome, the testimonial.]

[Formal recognition language — the award name, the hours, the period.]

[Closing — invite the volunteer forward, hand off to the certificate
presentation, or close with applause cue.]
```

Tone: warm but not sappy. Specific, not generic. The city is saying thank you, not running a marketing campaign. Avoid superlatives ("absolutely incredible," "one of the most amazing"). The specifics carry the warmth.

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**Naming and image consent:** Volunteers retain control of their name and likeness for public-facing recognition. The city's volunteer onboarding form should include a media-consent line; if it does not, ask the volunteer before publishing a social post or photo. A private letter does not require consent.

**Public records:** Recognition letters sent on city letterhead are public records under Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552. Drafts may be exempt under § 552.111 until issued. Spoken remarks delivered at an open meeting become part of the meeting record.

**Volunteer status:** Recognition does not change volunteer status, but if recognition could be interpreted as compensation (gift cards above a nominal value, paid trips), consult the city attorney and IRS de minimis fringe-benefit guidance before issuing.

**Equal recognition:** Avoid singling out one volunteer in a program where multiple volunteers contributed equally without acknowledging the team. Document the criteria so future recognitions are consistent.

**Retention:** Recognition letters retain per the Texas State Library Local Government Retention Schedule.
