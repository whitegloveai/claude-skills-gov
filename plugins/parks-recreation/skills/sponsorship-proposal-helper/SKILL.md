# Sponsorship Proposal Helper

> Drafts a sponsorship proposal a parks department can hand to a local business owner. Built for parks directors and special-events coordinators who need tiered benefits, honest reach metrics, and a restrictions section that survives council review.

## When to Use

- "Draft a sponsorship proposal for our July 4th festival."
- "We need tiered sponsorship packages for the youth soccer league."
- "Naming opportunity for the scoreboard at the new athletic complex — draft the pitch."
- "Local bank asked about presenting our summer concert series. Draft a proposal."
- "We're putting together a sponsorship deck for the fall harvest festival."
- Any moment the parks department is asking private-sector partners to support a program, event, or facility in exchange for recognition.

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- Sponsorship target (program, event, facility, scoreboard, or amenity)
- Sponsorship levels and dollar amounts at each
- Benefits at each level (signage, social, naming, hospitality, activation)
- Expected reach metrics — only what the department can document
- Term and renewal mechanics
- Restrictions (excluded categories; content guidelines)
- City approval pathway (staff, parks board, council)

Optional:

- Existing sponsor list and levels held
- Target list of businesses
- Anchor sponsor or matching-gift commitment
- Council resolution authorizing solicitation

If levels and benefits are not yet decided, ask before drafting. The skill will not invent levels.

## Process

1. Confirm the approval pathway. Naming rights or sponsorships above a stated threshold typically require council action.
2. Build the Opportunity Overview: factual description of the program/event, audience, dates, location, and why a local business benefits from association. Two paragraphs, not a sales pitch.
3. Build the Sponsorship Levels matrix from highest to lowest. Each level names price, benefits, and sponsor count limit.
4. Build Reach and Impact using only metrics the department can document. If this is a new event, say so.
5. Draft Recognition Mechanisms: where the logo appears, size and placement, duration, digital channels.
6. Draft Restrictions: excluded product categories and First Amendment-aware messaging guidelines.
7. Draft Approval Pathway: how the city accepts, signature authority, council action if any.
8. Add Term, Renewal, Out clause, and contact.
9. Add a disclaimer that the proposal is a solicitation of an offer, not a binding contract.

## Output Format

Plain markdown proposal, ready for design layout. No emoji.

```
SPONSORSHIP PROPOSAL
[Program / Event / Facility Name]
City of [City] Parks and Recreation
[Year or Season]

OPPORTUNITY OVERVIEW
[Two paragraphs. Paragraph 1: what the program/event is, the audience,
the dates, the location. Paragraph 2: why a local business benefits
from association.]

SPONSORSHIP LEVELS

Title — $[amount] (limit: 1)
- [4 top-tier benefits]

Premier — $[amount] (limit: [N])
- [3 mid-tier benefits]

Supporting — $[amount] (limit: [N])
- [2 benefits]

Friend — $[amount] (no limit)
- [2 entry benefits]

REACH AND IMPACT
- Past attendance, social impressions, media coverage, mailing list reach
[Use only metrics the department can document. Note "first year, no
history" where applicable.]

RECOGNITION MECHANISMS
- On-site signage, stage announcements, printed materials, digital
  channels, press release inclusion

TERM AND RENEWAL
[One event, one season, or multi-year. Right-of-first-refusal for
Title, escalator if any.]

RESTRICTIONS
The City of [City] does not accept sponsorship from: tobacco and
vaping, cannabis, sexually oriented businesses, political candidates
and PACs, predatory lending products, or any category inconsistent
with adopted City policy.

Sponsor messaging on City property is subject to the City's content
guidelines and the First Amendment. The City retains editorial control
over how sponsor recognition appears. No sponsor benefit grants
authority over programming, vendor selection, or City staff.

APPROVAL PATHWAY
- Staff: up to $[threshold]
- Parks Board: $[threshold] to $[threshold]
- City Council: above $[threshold] and any naming of a City facility
[Cite the applicable city code or council policy.]

OUT CLAUSE
Either party may terminate in writing with [N] days notice. The City
may terminate immediately if the sponsor becomes inconsistent with the
restrictions above; refund pro-rated.

CONTACT
[Coordinator name], [title] | [phone] | [email]
City of [City] Parks and Recreation | [city website]

This proposal is a solicitation of an offer, not a binding contract.
The agreement, if accepted, is documented separately and signed by
both parties.
```

Length: 500 to 800 words plus the benefits matrix. Tone: factual, plain, no fluff. Local sponsors are giving real money out of a real budget; the proposal should respect that.

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**Council approval and naming rights:** Naming rights and sponsorships of city facilities or programs commonly require council approval. Cities should adopt a written sponsorship and naming policy before soliciting Title-tier sponsors.

**First Amendment on city property:** Sponsor messaging on city property exists in a forum-analysis framework. The city must reserve editorial control to keep the channel from becoming a public forum it cannot then curate. The Restrictions section is the linchpin.

**Procurement:** Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 252 procurement rules generally do not cover sponsorship payments from the private sector to the city. Consult counsel where benefits given could be reframed as procurement (advertising buys, services provided by the sponsor).

**Public-private partnership:** Sponsorship at significant scale (long-term naming rights, facility operation, capital investment) may trigger Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 2267 (Public and Private Facilities and Infrastructure Act). Routine event sponsorship does not.

**Public records and tax-deductibility:** Executed agreements are public records under Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552. The city should not represent a sponsorship payment as tax-deductible; route donations through a 501(c)(3) friends-of-parks foundation if tax-deductibility is desired.
