# Municipal Voice and Tone Guide for Social Media

Guardrails for staff who run official city social media accounts. The goal is a voice that informs residents, holds up under public records review, and avoids the most common legal and political risks.

## The Voice in One Sentence

Neutral, helpful, civically appropriate, and grounded in facts and dates.

## What the Voice Sounds Like

- Direct verbs: "Council adopted," "Staff will host," "The library opens."
- Concrete numbers and dates: "$1.8 million," "10 a.m. Saturday," "14.3 miles of streets."
- Plain English: "We are repaving streets" beats "Pavement rehabilitation operations are underway."
- Acknowledges impact: "Construction is disruptive. Here's what to expect."
- Department-appropriate warmth: the library can sound friendly; the police account should stay neutral; the fire account should be reassuring; public works should be operational.

## What the Voice Avoids

- Superlatives: "amazing," "groundbreaking," "world-class," "incredible," "best-in-class."
- Marketing copy: "Don't miss this incredible opportunity!"
- Hot takes, snark, or jokes about other governments, businesses, or residents.
- Endorsements of candidates, businesses, products, or partisan causes.
- Editorial framing of contested council votes ("Council made the right call to...").
- Emoji-heavy posts on substantive city business. Light use on parks, library, and event posts is acceptable per city brand voice.
- AI-coded constructions ("It is not just X, but Y," em-dash overuse, "in today's fast-paced world").

## Hard Guardrails

### 1. No Endorsements

The city's accounts do not endorse candidates, ballot measures, partisan causes, or commercial products. This includes "soft" endorsements (retweeting candidate posts, liking endorsement posts, tagging candidates positively in city posts).

For city employees in covered positions, the federal Hatch Act may apply if any portion of their salary is federally funded; covered employees may not engage in partisan political activity using government accounts. Texas Election Code § 255.003 prohibits the use of public funds for political advertising on bond, charter, or candidate elections.

When a vendor, partner, or nonprofit asks for an endorsement post: politely decline. The city can announce a partnership, recognize a milestone, or thank a donor for a specific contribution. The city does not endorse.

### 2. Neutrality on Contested Council Matters

For contested council votes, contested public hearings, ongoing personnel matters, or active litigation: report what the council did, when, and the vote count. Do not characterize the merits.

Acceptable: "Council voted 5-2 to approve the rezoning at 1100 Pecan Street. Full minutes are at cedarridgetx.gov/council."

Not acceptable: "Council made the responsible choice tonight on Pecan Street."

If an account holder feels strongly about the matter, that account holder should not draft the post. Route it.

### 3. Do Not Respond to Defamation in Thread

A comment alleging that a named staff member, council member, or vendor engaged in wrongdoing may be defamatory. The city's response in-thread will not correct the record and may amplify the reach of the comment.

The protocol:
1. Screenshot the comment with timestamp and the surrounding thread.
2. Preserve the post in the city's social media archive.
3. Route the screenshot and post URL to the City Attorney.
4. Do not respond in-thread.
5. Do not delete unless the moderation policy clearly authorizes (e.g., the comment contains slurs, threats, or doxxing).

### 4. Moderation Is a Forum Question

Official city accounts that allow public comment likely create a limited public forum under the First Amendment. *Lindke v. Freed* (2024) and prior cases (*Davison v. Randall*, *Knight v. Trump*) confirm that viewpoint-based moderation of citizen comments is legally risky.

The city should have a written, viewpoint-neutral social media moderation policy, posted publicly, that defines:
- What categories of content may be removed (threats, doxxing, obscenity, off-topic spam, copyright violations).
- The criteria for blocking a user (typically: repeated, documented policy violations).
- A documented review process before any deletion or block.

Every moderation action should be logged. Apply the policy uniformly.

### 5. Public Records Implications

Every post, comment, direct message, and moderation action on an official city account is a public record under Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552. Retention is governed by Texas State Library Local Government Retention Schedule GR (General Records), Item 1050-26 (Social Media Records).

Use an archiving tool (ArchiveSocial, Hootsuite, Smarsh, or equivalent) for every official account. Do not run city business from personal accounts. Do not direct-message residents from personal accounts on city matters; if it happens, the conversation is still a public record and the city is obligated to retain it.

A TPIA request for "all social media posts and comments related to X" is a routine request. The city must be able to produce it.

### 6. Crisis Communications

For crisis posts (boil-water, severe weather, public safety incident, infrastructure failure, cyber incident): use the crisis-comms-template skill for the holding statement and update language. Do not improvise crisis messaging on social. Verify facts with the incident command before posting.

### 7. Personnel and Litigation

Do not post about active personnel matters, internal investigations, or pending litigation beyond a neutral statement that the city does not comment on personnel or pending litigation. Route any draft post that touches these areas to the City Attorney and HR before posting.

### 8. Image and ADA Accessibility

- Provide alt text on every image. Auto-generated alt text is not sufficient.
- Use camelCase or PascalCase hashtags (#CedarRidgeTX, not #cedarridgetx) so screen readers parse them correctly.
- Captions on video must be reviewed; auto-generated captions are starting points, not finished captions.
- Avoid text-only-in-image posts; if a graphic carries information, that information must also appear in the post body.

## When to Escalate to Legal

Send the post and the situation to the City Attorney before posting if:
- The post touches active litigation, including potential litigation (a notice of claim, a demand letter, a discrimination charge, a TPIA dispute).
- The post involves a named employee, council member, or vendor in a way that could imply wrongdoing.
- The post is in response to a defamatory or hostile thread.
- The post addresses an officer-involved incident, in-custody death, or any matter where the city may have liability exposure.
- The post addresses a personnel termination, complaint, or investigation.
- The post discusses a code enforcement matter involving a specific named property or owner.

## Quick Reference: Decision Checklist Before Posting

1. Is the content factually verified? (numbers, dates, names, citations)
2. Is the tone neutral and civically appropriate?
3. Does the post avoid endorsement, partisan framing, and editorializing?
4. Is the image alt-texted and accessible?
5. Are the hashtags camelCase or PascalCase?
6. Is a Spanish version or summary needed?
7. Has the relevant department head reviewed?
8. For contested or risk-flagged content: has the City Attorney reviewed?
9. Is the post archived through the city's archiving tool?
10. Is the AI-assistance notation documented per city policy?

## Final Note

A municipal social account that is boring, accurate, and consistent is doing its job. An entertaining account that creates legal exposure or public records headaches is not.
