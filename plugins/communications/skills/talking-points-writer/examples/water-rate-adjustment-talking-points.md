# Example: Water Rate Adjustment Talking Points

This is a worked example for the talking-points-writer skill. It uses the fictional City of Cedar Ridge, TX. The proposed water rate adjustment is the subject of the council-ops decision-memo-writer example. PIO Marcia Delgado and Chief of Staff have prepared talking points for council members and the mayor before the August 11, 2026 council meeting where the rate ordinance will receive its first reading.

## Input

- **Topic:** Proposed FY2027 water rate adjustment (average residential bill increases by $4.80 per month, weighted toward higher-volume users)
- **Position:** Staff is recommending; council has not yet voted; the August 11 meeting is the first reading
- **Audience:** Constituents (ahead of the August 11 hearing); media (KCRG, Cedar Ridge Gazette)
- **Setting:** Council meetings, town hall, possible media interviews
- **Factual baseline:**
  - System operates at a $2.3M annual operating deficit currently subsidized from the General Fund
  - $42M in mandatory infrastructure replacement over the next 10 years (cast-iron mains, two ground storage tanks, TCEQ-compliance upgrades)
  - The rate adjustment closes the operating deficit by year 3 and funds 60% of the 10-year capital plan
  - Average residential customer (5,000 gallons/month) sees $4.80/month increase
  - High-volume residential (15,000+ gallons/month) sees larger increase, weighted on conservation principles
  - Low-volume customers (under 3,000 gallons/month) see minimal increase
  - Lifeline rate structure for qualifying low-income households (under 60% of area median income) reduces base charge by 50%
- **Known anticipated questions:** Why now? Why not bond it? What about seniors? What about renters? Why is the water authority paying its general manager so much? Have you cut costs first?
- **Spokesperson:** Mayor Castillo for media; council members individually for constituent contacts
- **Bilingual:** Spanish summary needed

## Output

```
TALKING POINTS — FY2027 WATER RATE ADJUSTMENT

For: Mayor Castillo (media); Cedar Ridge City Council members
(constituent contacts)
Setting: August 11, 2026 council meeting (first reading), constituent
contacts, possible media interviews
Audience: Constituents, KCRG, Cedar Ridge Gazette
Date: August 4, 2026
Prepared by: Marcia Delgado, Public Information Officer
Position: Staff recommendation pending first reading. Council members
should describe their own positions; the city's position is "staff is
recommending, council will decide after public hearing."

---

30-SECOND SUMMARY

Cedar Ridge's water system has been running a $2.3 million annual
operating deficit, which the General Fund has been covering instead
of using that money for parks, streets, and public safety. Over the
next ten years, we have $42 million in mandatory infrastructure
replacement — cast-iron mains that are past their service life, two
ground storage tanks, and TCEQ compliance upgrades. Staff is
recommending a rate adjustment that closes the operating deficit by
year three and funds about 60 percent of the ten-year capital plan.
For the average household, that's $4.80 a month. We've built in a
lifeline rate for low-income households and structured the increase
so that conservation pays off. Council will hold a public hearing on
August 11 before deciding.

MAIN MESSAGE POINTS

1. The current rate does not cover the cost of running the system.
   - The water utility runs a $2.3 million annual operating deficit
     subsidized from the General Fund.
   - That subsidy means roughly $80 per Cedar Ridge household per year
     in property tax dollars going to water operations instead of to
     parks, streets, or public safety.

2. Cedar Ridge has $42 million in mandatory infrastructure
   replacement over the next ten years.
   - Cast-iron mains in older neighborhoods are past service life.
   - Two ground storage tanks need rehabilitation or replacement under
     TCEQ rules.
   - Without rate revenue, the only option is more debt or deferred
     maintenance — which is how we got here.

3. The proposed adjustment is weighted toward higher-volume users.
   - Average residential bill ($4.80/month) is below 3 percent of a
     typical household water bill.
   - Customers using over 15,000 gallons per month see a larger
     percentage increase, which rewards conservation.
   - Customers using under 3,000 gallons per month see minimal
     increase.

4. There is a lifeline rate for low-income households.
   - Households at or below 60 percent of area median income qualify
     for a 50 percent reduction in the base monthly charge.
   - Application is one page; renewable annually; verified through
     existing CDBG income documentation if households are already
     enrolled in other assistance programs.
   - We are working with United Way of Caddo County to enroll eligible
     residents.

5. This council has consistently chosen long-term reliability over
   short-term comfort.
   - The 2024 public safety bond, the FY2026 street overlay program,
     and now the water capital plan are pieces of the same approach.
   - Deferring this further costs more later and risks service
     interruptions or TCEQ enforcement.

ANTICIPATED Q&A

Q: Why now? You just raised taxes last year.
A: Water and property tax are different funds with different purposes.
The General Fund has been subsidizing water operations, which is
neither legal in the long run nor fair to ratepayers in other utilities
who pay full cost of service. We've put off the water adjustment for
five years. The longer we wait, the steeper the catch-up.

Q: Why not just bond the capital projects?
A: The rate plan does include a debt component for the largest
projects. But operations have to be covered by rates, not debt. You
can't bond your way out of an operating deficit; that just shifts
costs to future ratepayers with interest.

Q: What about seniors on fixed incomes?
A: The lifeline rate is available to households at or below 60 percent
of area median income, which captures most seniors on Social Security
alone in Cedar Ridge. We're partnering with the senior center and
United Way to help with applications.

Q: What about renters who don't pay the water bill directly?
A: Most apartment renters pay water through their landlord, and rate
adjustments can flow into rent. We've shared the rate proposal with
the multifamily property owners' group and asked them to keep
adjustments proportional. The city does not regulate rent, but the
council will continue to monitor.

Q: Has the city cut costs first?
A: The utility has held operating expenses below the consumer price
index for the last four years. Personnel costs are below the
benchmark of comparable Texas utilities. Staff identified $480,000 in
operating efficiencies in the FY2027 budget proposal. We've cut what
we can cut without affecting safety or reliability.

Q: Will this go to a public vote?
A: Water rates are set by ordinance, not by election. The first
reading is August 11. Public hearing is at the August 25 meeting.
Final vote is anticipated September 8. Anyone may speak at the public
hearing.

Q: What happens if council says no?
A: Then the General Fund continues to subsidize water operations,
which means less for streets and parks, and the capital plan slips,
which means more deferred maintenance. The TCEQ infrastructure
requirements don't go away. Staff would come back with a smaller
adjustment proposal, but the math doesn't work indefinitely.

Q: Why does the regional water authority's general manager make so
much?
A: Cedar Ridge does not set salaries at the regional water authority.
Cedar Ridge is one of four member cities. Our wholesale water purchase
contract is set through that authority's board, on which Council
Member Tran represents Cedar Ridge. Concerns about authority
governance should be directed to that board.

PIVOT PHRASES

- "What I'd add to that is what this rate plan actually protects..."
- "The bigger picture here is the choice between paying now or paying
  more later..."
- "Let me speak to what Cedar Ridge families will actually see on
  their bill..."
- "I think the more important question is what happens if we don't
  act..."
- "Where this council has been consistent is choosing reliability over
  cosmetic numbers..."

BRIDGES TO OTHER PRIORITIES

- "This connects to the broader work we've been doing on long-term
  infrastructure — public safety bond, streets, and now water."
- "It's worth noting that the same council that adopted the street
  overlay program is asking the same hard questions on water."
- "The lifeline rate is part of how this council has approached equity
  across services, alongside the senior tax exemption and the library
  fine-free policy."

WHAT TO AVOID

- Do not characterize the merits of the rate adjustment before the
  vote — describe staff recommendation only.
- Do not commit to a vote outcome before the August 11 first reading.
- Do not name specific opponents or advocates in public remarks.
- Do not compare Cedar Ridge rates favorably or unfavorably to
  specific neighboring cities. The systems are not directly
  comparable.
- Do not discuss the regional water authority's personnel matters or
  pending governance disputes; refer to Council Member Tran.
- Do not speculate on how individual council members will vote.
- Do not suggest that opponents of the rate adjustment are
  uninformed; treat opposition concerns as serious and answer them.

EN ESPANOL — RESUMEN

La Ciudad de Cedar Ridge esta considerando un ajuste a las tarifas de
agua para el ano fiscal 2027. El aumento promedio para un hogar
residencial es de $4.80 al mes. Los hogares de bajos ingresos pueden
calificar para una tarifa reducida del 50 por ciento en el cargo
mensual base. La audiencia publica es el 25 de agosto. El primer
lectura del ordenanza es el 11 de agosto. Para mas informacion, visite
cedarridgetx.gov/water o llame al (555) 555-0140.

ATTRIBUTION AND VERIFICATION

Operating deficit ($2.3M) and 10-year capital figure ($42M) verified
against the FY2027 Water Fund proposed budget and the 2025 Water
Utility Master Plan, August 1, 2026. Rate calculations verified
against the rate study by Raftelis Financial Consultants, July 2026.
City Attorney has reviewed for litigation exposure related to the
2024 ratepayer challenge; talking points conform to the agreed
litigation messaging.

---
AI-assisted draft, reviewed by Marcia Delgado, Public Information
Officer, and the City Attorney.
```

## Notes for the User

- Distribute individually to council members; do not group-email in a way that creates a walking-quorum risk under Tex. Gov't Code § 551.001.
- Council members may add their own positions to the basic framing. Staff cannot direct elected officials on their positions.
- The Spanish summary should be confirmed by the city's Spanish-language reviewer (typically the Library Director's bilingual programming coordinator at Cedar Ridge) before any media distribution.
- Coordinate with the finance procurement-rfp-builder for the underlying rate study procurement documentation if media or council asks about the consultant selection.
- After the August 11 first reading, refresh the talking points to reflect council comments and any amendments before the August 25 public hearing.
- Archive the final version in the council packet folder; if a TPIA request is made, coordinate with the City Secretary on the § 552.111 deliberative process exemption analysis.
