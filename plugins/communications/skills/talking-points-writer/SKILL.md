# Talking Points Writer

> Drafts disciplined, briefing-quality talking points for elected officials and department heads on contested or high-stakes topics. Built for the PIOs, chiefs of staff, and city managers who prep the people speaking publicly.

## When to Use

- "Mayor has a KCRG interview tomorrow on the water rate increase."
- "Council members need talking points before Tuesday's rezoning vote."
- "City Manager is testifying at the regional transit board next week."
- "Chief is doing a town hall on the body camera policy."
- "Mayor's going to a chamber lunch and will get budget questions."
- An elected official or department head is about to speak publicly on a contested topic.
- A contested vote or hearing is approaching and council members want consistent framing.

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- Topic in one sentence
- Position: adopted city position, neutral framing, or council-member-specific
- Audience: constituents, media, regional body, chamber, neighborhood
- Setting: council meeting, interview, town hall, panel, testimony
- Factual baseline: dollar amounts, dates, vote history, citations, expected impact

Optional:

- Known anticipated questions from press, advocates, or meeting commenters
- Spokesperson's name and prior public statements
- Time limit on remarks; live vs. written Q&A
- Bilingual flag

If position, audience, or factual baseline is missing, ask before drafting. Do not draft on contested matters without confirming the adopted position or neutral framing.

## Process

1. Confirm the position. If council has adopted one, that is the message. If pending, draft neutral framing only and flag what the spokesperson should not say.
2. Confirm audience and setting. Town halls differ from media interviews and regional testimony.
3. Draft the 30-second summary — the paragraph the spokesperson can deliver from memory. Lead with human impact, not process.
4. Draft 3–5 main message points. Each has a headline assertion and one or two supporting facts. Points must be repeatable.
5. Build Anticipated Q&A. 5–8 likely questions with concise responses. For tough questions: acknowledge, fact, bridge.
6. Write Pivot Phrases — short transitions to redirect to main messages.
7. Write Bridges to Other Priorities — sentences that connect the topic to broader city priorities.
8. Write What to Avoid — topics, phrasing, or named individuals that should not come up. Be direct.

## Output Format

Plain markdown. Internal briefing document, not public release. May be subject to TPIA disclosure if not exempt under § 552.111.

```
TALKING POINTS — [TOPIC]

For: [Spokesperson name and title]
Setting: [Council meeting / Interview / Town hall / Testimony]
Audience: [Constituents / Media / Regional body / etc.]
Date: [DATE]
Prepared by: [Name, Title]
Position: [Adopted city position OR neutral framing — pending]

---

30-SECOND SUMMARY
[150–200 word paragraph the spokesperson can deliver from memory.
Leads with human impact, not process.]

MAIN MESSAGE POINTS
1. [Headline assertion]
   - Supporting fact: [number, date, citation]
   - Supporting fact: [number, date, citation]
[Up to 5 points. Each must be repeatable.]

ANTICIPATED Q&A
Q: [Likely question, phrased as it will be asked]
A: [Concise response. For tough questions: acknowledge, fact, bridge.]
[5–8 pairs.]

PIVOT PHRASES (to return to main message)
- "What I'd add to that is..."
- "The bigger picture here is..."
- "Where this council has been consistent is..."

BRIDGES TO OTHER PRIORITIES
- "This connects to the broader work we've been doing on..."
- "It's worth noting that [related project or program]..."

WHAT TO AVOID
- [Topic, phrasing, or named individual to steer away from]
- Speculation about pending litigation, personnel, or future votes
- Comparisons to other cities or candidates that could read as
  endorsement or criticism
- Never characterize the merits of an active executive session matter
- Never speculate on a vote not yet taken
- Never name an employee who is the subject of personnel action

ATTRIBUTION AND VERIFICATION
Numbers, dates, and citations verified against [source] on [date].
City Attorney has reviewed for litigation exposure on [date].

---
AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [Name, Title].
```

Length: 600–1,200 words depending on topic complexity.

Tone: confident, factual, respectful of the audience. Not defensive. Not promotional. Acknowledges concerns plainly.

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**Public records and § 552.111:** Staff-prepared talking points for an elected official are typically agency memoranda. Drafts may be exempt from disclosure under Tex. Gov't Code § 552.111 (deliberative process). Once an official has spoken publicly from the points, exemption may be lost. Treat every talking-points document as if it could be released.

**Hatch Act and political activity:** Federal Hatch Act limits partisan activity for employees in federally funded positions. Tex. Elec. Code § 255.003 prohibits public funds for political advertising. Avoid endorsement of candidates and partisan framing of ballot measures.

**Active litigation:** Do not include statements that could be admissions against the city's interest in pending litigation. Route any talking points touching litigation through the City Attorney before distribution.

**Personnel matters:** Talking points discussing a named employee or active personnel investigation must be reviewed by HR and the City Attorney. The spokesperson should typically confirm the existence of a process and decline further comment.

**Texas Open Meetings Act:** Council members may discuss talking points individually with staff but must not deliberate among themselves outside a posted meeting (Tex. Gov't Code § 551.001 walking-quorum prohibition). Distribute neutrally, not as a poll.

**Plain language:** Use 8th-grade reading level. Test by reading aloud — if the spokesperson sounds like a press release, rewrite.

**Bilingual delivery:** Prepare parallel Spanish talking points where the jurisdiction warrants. Machine translation alone is not sufficient for high-stakes talking points.
