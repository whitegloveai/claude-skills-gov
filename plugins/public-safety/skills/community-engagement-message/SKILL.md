# Community Engagement Message

> Drafts a community-facing message from a public safety agency. Built for chiefs, PIOs, and command staff communicating with residents on policy, enforcement focus, or safety topics outside of an active crisis.

## When to Use

- "Draft a message announcing our school-zone enforcement focus next month."
- "Write the announcement for the new co-responder mental health program."
- "We need a community message on the increased pedestrian fatalities in District 3."
- "Recognize the citizens who helped at the apartment fire on Cypress."
- "Reminder for residents about firework restrictions ahead of July 4."
- The agency wants to share information that is operational, not a crisis, with the community.
- The message will be posted on social media, sent through CodeRED, included in a newsletter, or read at a council meeting.

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- Topic and the operational reason for communicating now.
- Agency and channel (police social, fire newsletter, joint city message).
- Target audience (residents citywide, residents of a specific neighborhood, parents, business owners).
- Key facts the agency wants communicated.
- Tone target (informational, cautionary, celebratory).
- Named sender authority (Chief, Captain, PIO).

Optional:

- Prior comms on the same topic, for continuity.
- Bilingual requirement (operational standard for any service-critical message in LEP neighborhoods).
- Open records exposure (any topic that may generate TPIA requests, e.g., a use-of-force update, should be coordinated with the City Attorney).

If the topic touches an active investigation, an officer-involved incident, or pending litigation, ask before drafting. Those topics are not community-engagement content.

## Process

1. Confirm the topic is not crisis (otherwise route to `crisis-comms-template`) and not investigation-active.
2. Identify a single message goal. "Inform, then invite engagement" is the default. Avoid multi-purpose messages; they read as marketing.
3. Draft the Headline. Plain, specific, no buzzwords. "School Zone Enforcement Focus, March 2026" beats "Keeping Our Kids Safe."
4. Draft the Opening. One short paragraph that humanizes the issue without sensationalizing it. Lead with the citizen, not the agency.
5. Draft the Plain-Language Explanation. Two to four short paragraphs. Define any technical or legal terms inline. Cite controlling authority where useful, plainly.
6. Draft How This Affects Citizens. Concrete, specific, behavioral. "Drivers in the four marked school zones will see increased patrol and may receive warnings the first week, citations after."
7. Draft How To Engage. Specific touchpoints: a meeting, a phone number, a ride-along program, a community survey, a PTA event.
8. Draft the Sign-off. Named senior leader. Use the same name and title the community already knows.
9. Pass the First Amendment check: no commentary on pending cases, no characterization of individuals not yet adjudicated, no editorializing about ongoing litigation.
10. Bilingual: produce English and Spanish where required, reviewed by a fluent reviewer.

## Output Format

Plain markdown. One- to two-page document, suitable for cross-posting to social, newsletter, and the city website.

```
[HEADLINE — plain, specific, no buzzwords]

OPENING
[One short paragraph. Humanize the issue. Lead with the citizen.]

WHAT THIS IS
[Two to four short paragraphs of plain-language explanation. Define
technical or legal terms inline. Cite controlling authority where it
helps the reader understand.]

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOU
[Concrete, specific behavioral guidance. Bullets if appropriate.]
- [Behavior the citizen may see]
- [Behavior the citizen may want to take]
- [What to expect from the agency]

HOW TO ENGAGE
[Named, specific touchpoints. Include contact and timing.]
- [Touchpoint 1, date/time/location]
- [Touchpoint 2, date/time/location]
- [Ride-along, survey, ask-the-chief, neighborhood meeting]

[Sign-off]
[Name, Title]
[Police Department / Fire Department / OEM]

En espanol:
[Spanish translation of the full message, reviewed by [reviewer name].]

AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [Name, Title].
```

Length: 300 to 700 words English; Spanish translation typically of comparable length.

Tone: sober, factual, warm without being saccharine. Public safety communicates trust by being specific, not by being friendly.

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**Channel-level disclosure:** If this message is later delivered through an AI-driven interactive channel (an IVR system that uses an AI voice, a chatbot embedded on the city website, an SMS bot), Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051 disclosure attaches at the channel level. The agency must disclose to the citizen that they are interacting with AI. The message text drafted here is not the trigger; the delivery channel is.

**First Amendment:** Police social media is a designated public forum if the agency opens comments. Avoid commentary on pending cases, individual officers under investigation, or pending litigation. Coordinate with the City Attorney before any message that could be read as a statement on a pending matter.

**Garrity:** If quoting an officer about a personnel matter, *Garrity v. New Jersey*, 385 U.S. 493 (1967) and Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 614 apply. Do not include compelled statements from internal affairs investigations in community messaging.

**Public records (Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552):** Community messages are public records. Drafts may be exempt under § 552.111 until released. Comments and engagement responses on official channels are also records and must be retained.

**ADA / language access:** Web posts need alt text and readable contrast. Spanish translation is operationally mandatory in LEP neighborhoods.

**Retention:** Public messaging and social media posts retain per Texas State Library Local Government Retention Schedule GR, Item 1000-58 (Public Information).
