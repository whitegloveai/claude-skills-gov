# Worked Example: 4th Amendment Vehicle Consent Search Bulletin

This example shows a fictional Cedar Ridge PD training bulletin on consent searches of vehicles, drafted in response to two recent Fourth Amendment cases that have changed how the agency approaches consent during traffic stops. The bulletin was authored by Sgt. James Holloway under the supervision of Lt. Anika Patel and signed by Chief Eduardo Vega.

---

```
CITY OF CEDAR RIDGE POLICE DEPARTMENT
TRAINING BULLETIN TB-2026-007
DISTRIBUTION: All sworn personnel, patrol and CID
EFFECTIVE: March 24, 2026
SUPERSEDES: TB-2023-014 (Consent Searches — Vehicles)
CONTROLLING AUTHORITY: U.S. Const. amend. IV; Caniglia v. Strom,
593 U.S. 194 (2021); Lange v. California, 594 U.S. 295 (2021);
Schneckloth v. Bustamonte, 412 U.S. 218 (1973); Tex. Code Crim.
Proc. art. 14.03–14.04; CRPD General Order 4-12 (Search and Seizure)

TITLE: Consent Searches of Vehicles — Scope, Withdrawal, and Passengers

LEARNING OBJECTIVE
By the end of this roll-call training, all sworn personnel will be
able to:
1. Articulate the three elements of valid consent (voluntary,
   knowing, and given by a person with authority).
2. Identify the scope-limiting language a driver can use and
   recognize when consent has been withdrawn.
3. Distinguish between consent given by a driver versus a passenger
   and identify when a passenger has standing to refuse a search of
   their own belongings.

BACKGROUND
Consent is the most common exception to the Fourth Amendment's
warrant requirement, and it is also the most often mishandled. Two
recent Supreme Court decisions have tightened how courts evaluate
the scope of consent and the lawful boundaries of warrantless
intrusion.

In Caniglia v. Strom, the Court rejected a "community caretaking"
exception for warrantless entries into the home, signaling a narrower
view of officer-initiated warrantless intrusion generally. While
Caniglia is a home case, courts have read it as a reminder that
exceptions to the warrant requirement are not to be expanded by
analogy.

Lange v. California held that the hot pursuit of a fleeing
misdemeanor suspect does not categorically justify a warrantless
entry. Lange reinforces that even in vehicle contexts, expansion
beyond the express scope of an exception (here, consent) will be
challenged.

For consent searches of vehicles, the established standard under
Schneckloth v. Bustamonte still controls: consent must be voluntary,
knowing, and given by a person with authority. CRPD General Order
4-12 has been updated to reflect the points in this bulletin.

PROCEDURE
1. Before requesting consent, complete the lawful purpose of the
   stop. If the stop has been completed and there is no reasonable
   suspicion to extend, advise the driver they are free to leave
   before initiating a consensual encounter.
2. Request consent in plain, unambiguous language. Example: "Do I
   have your permission to search the vehicle?" Document the
   driver's response verbatim on body-worn camera and in the
   supplemental.
3. Confirm the scope. If the driver consents to a search of the
   trunk, that does not extend to a locked compartment or to a
   passenger's bag. Ask separately for any compartment or container
   whose scope is not clear.
4. Monitor for withdrawal. Statements such as "I changed my mind,"
   "That's enough," "Stop," or any physical attempt to close a
   compartment constitute withdrawal. On withdrawal, terminate the
   search and document.
5. Passenger consent is limited to the passenger's own belongings
   and areas under the passenger's control. A passenger cannot
   consent to a search of the driver's locked compartment. A
   driver cannot consent to a search of a passenger's purse or
   backpack.
6. If consent is refused, do not condition release on consent. Do
   not extend the stop without independent reasonable suspicion.

DOCUMENTATION REQUIREMENTS
- Body-worn camera activated before consent is requested and left
  on through the full search.
- Supplemental report must include: the verbatim request, the
  verbatim response, the scope stated by the driver, any
  withdrawal, and the result.
- RMS field "Consent Search" set to Yes/No with the basis stated.

COMMON MISTAKES AND WATCH-OUTS
- Treating "OK" or "sure" as full-scope consent without confirming
  the scope.
- Continuing to search after a clear withdrawal statement because
  "they didn't really mean it."
- Searching a passenger's bag on the driver's consent.
- Extending the stop past its purpose to develop reasonable
  suspicion; the Supreme Court has been clear in Rodriguez v.
  United States, 575 U.S. 348 (2015) that this is unlawful.
- Asking for consent in a way that conveys the driver is not free
  to refuse (number of officers, positioning, tone).

ROLL-CALL DISCUSSION POINTS
1. The driver says "Yeah, I guess so." How do you proceed?
2. The driver consents to a search of the trunk. You see a locked
   metal box inside the trunk. What can you do without further
   consent or a separate exception?
3. A passenger has a backpack on the floorboard. The driver
   consents. Can you search the backpack? What if the backpack is
   on the driver's seat?
4. The driver consents and three minutes in says, "Hey, are we
   almost done?" Is that withdrawal?
```
