# Resolution Drafter

> Drafts a council resolution in proper form. Built for council members and staff who need to deliver a polished resolution without spending an afternoon on whereas clauses.

## When to Use

- "Draft a resolution honoring [person] on their retirement."
- "I need a resolution opposing the proposed state legislation on [topic]."
- "Build me a resolution authorizing the City Manager to apply for the [X] grant."
- "Draft a resolution endorsing the County's regional transportation plan."
- A council member wants a one-page resolution on the next agenda and needs the standard form.

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- Purpose of the resolution. One of:
  - **Recognition / honor** (a person, an organization, an anniversary)
  - **Position statement** (support for or opposition to legislation, a position on a regional issue)
  - **Intergovernmental request** (asking another body to take action)
  - **Authorization** (authorizing the City Manager or staff to take a specific act, such as applying for a grant or executing a routine document)
- Subject of the resolution (who or what)
- Key facts and findings the council should make (the predicates that go in the whereas clauses)
- Desired action / operative language

Optional:

- Sponsoring council member(s)
- Resolution number (if pre-assigned by the City Secretary; otherwise leave as `[NUMBER]`)
- Effective or "passed and approved" date
- Whether the city charter requires posting or two readings for resolutions (rare; most charters allow single-reading adoption)

## Process

1. Confirm this is a resolution, not an ordinance. Resolutions express council position, recognize people, or authorize specific routine acts. They do not change the city code, levy taxes, or enact penalties — those require ordinances.
2. Draft the title. Resolutions are titled by their purpose (e.g., "A Resolution of the City Council of the City of [Your City] Honoring …").
3. Draft the whereas clauses. Each is a single sentence stating a factual or legal predicate. Clauses build from general to specific. Avoid editorializing; cite to public records or laws where possible.
4. Draft the now-therefore-be-it-resolved clauses. These are the operative language — what the council is actually doing.
5. Add the passed-and-approved date and signature blocks for the mayor and city secretary.
6. Add a "Approved as to form" signature line for the city attorney.
7. Use plain capitalization (no all-caps for emphasis except in section headers and signatures).
8. Verify the operative language uses resolution verbs (*resolves*, *expresses*, *requests*, *authorizes*, *commends*) — not ordinance verbs (*enacts*, *amends the code*, *prohibits*, *establishes a penalty*).

## Output Format

```
RESOLUTION NO. [NUMBER]

A RESOLUTION OF THE CITY COUNCIL OF THE CITY OF [NAME], TEXAS,
[ONE-LINE STATEMENT OF PURPOSE]; AND PROVIDING FOR AN EFFECTIVE DATE.

WHEREAS, [first predicate, factual or legal];

WHEREAS, [next predicate];

WHEREAS, [final predicate connecting predicates to the desired action];

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED BY THE CITY COUNCIL OF THE CITY OF
[NAME], TEXAS:

Section 1. The City Council [recognizes / supports / opposes /
authorizes] [operative language].

Section 2. [Additional operative language, if any.]

Section 3. This Resolution shall take effect immediately upon its
passage.

PASSED AND APPROVED this ___ day of __________, 20__, at a
[regular / special] meeting of the City Council of the City of [Name],
Texas, by a vote of ___ ayes, ___ nays, and ___ abstentions.

____________________________________
[Mayor], Mayor

ATTEST:

____________________________________
[City Secretary], City Secretary

APPROVED AS TO FORM:

____________________________________
[City Attorney], City Attorney
```

Length: typically one page, sometimes two for complex authorization resolutions.

Tone: formal, ceremonial where appropriate (recognition resolutions can warm in tone but should not be effusive), neutral on contested matters.

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**Resolution vs. Ordinance — important:**

- A resolution expresses council position or authorizes a single act. It does not change the city code.
- An ordinance changes the city code, levies taxes, establishes regulations, or imposes penalties.
- A resolution that purports to do what only an ordinance can do is voidable.
- A few items that look like resolutions are actually ordinances under Texas law (e.g., adopting the budget — a budget *can* be adopted by resolution in some general-law cities but most home-rule charters require an ordinance).

**Texas Open Meetings Act (Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 551):** The agenda must specifically describe the resolution being considered. Most charters allow adoption on a single reading. Confirm the city's own charter rule.

**Public Records (Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552):** The adopted resolution is a permanent public record. Drafts in working channels are typically exempt under § 552.111 until adopted.

**Retention:** Adopted resolutions retain permanently (Texas State Library Local Government Retention Schedule GR, Item 1000-23).

**Use of city resources for political resolutions:** Resolutions opposing or supporting specific candidates are not appropriate. Resolutions taking a policy position on legislation are generally permissible but should be reviewed by the City Attorney where the position relates to a contested election or campaign (Tex. Elec. Code § 255.003 limits use of public funds for political advertising).
