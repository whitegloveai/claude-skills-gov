# Decision Memo Writer

> Drafts a clear, council-ready decision memo. Built for staff who need to present a contested decision in a format that respects council's time and supports an informed vote.

## When to Use

- "Draft a decision memo on the proposed water rate adjustment with three options."
- "I need a council memo laying out the options for the senior center site selection."
- "Build me a memo on the curbside compost pilot — three implementation options, fiscal impact each."
- "Help me structure a recommendation memo on the policing reform package."
- A department lead is preparing a contested item for the agenda and needs a structured memo for the packet.

## Inputs Needed

Required:

- Decision topic (one or two sentences)
- Background — why is this in front of council, what triggered it
- Issue statement — the precise question council is being asked to answer
- Options being analyzed (typically 2 to 4)
- For each option: a one-line description, fiscal impact, legal/regulatory considerations, and implementation timeline
- Staff's recommendation (or an explicit "no staff position" if appropriate)
- Requested council action (the motion language council will vote on)

Optional:

- Stakeholder input received to date (boards/commissions, public meetings, written comments)
- Equity / disparate impact analysis
- Sustainability or environmental considerations
- Comparison to peer cities
- Any public hearings or notice requirements that must be satisfied before adoption

## Process

1. Confirm the issue statement is a single, answerable question. If it is two questions, split the memo or pick the primary one.
2. Confirm the options are mutually distinguishable. "Adopt the staff recommendation" and "Approve as drafted" are not separate options.
3. Draft the standard memo header (TO/FROM/DATE/SUBJECT). Use the city manager's office's standard form if known.
4. Draft Background — 1 to 3 paragraphs. Keep it factual.
5. State the Issue. One sentence ending in a question mark.
6. For each Option, draft a balanced section with these subheadings: Description, Pros, Cons, Fiscal Impact, Legal/Regulatory Considerations, Implementation Timeline. Length per option: half a page to one page.
7. Draft the Recommendation. State the recommended option, the reason, and any caveats. If staff is taking no position, say so explicitly.
8. Draft Requested Council Action. Provide motion language council can read into the record.
9. List Attachments at the end (drafts, public comment summaries, fiscal modeling).
10. Reread for tone: factual, neutral on the contested elements until the Recommendation section. Avoid loaded adjectives.

## Output Format

```
TO:        [Mayor and Council, or Council subcommittee]
FROM:      [Author name and title]
DATE:      [Date the memo is finalized]
SUBJECT:   [Topic — concise, ≤ 12 words]

BACKGROUND
[1–3 paragraphs of factual context. Why is this in front of council?
What triggered it? What is the current state?]

ISSUE
[Single sentence ending in a question mark.]

OPTIONS

Option 1: [Title]
Description: [1–3 sentences.]
Pros:
  - [Pro]
  - [Pro]
Cons:
  - [Con]
  - [Con]
Fiscal Impact: [Year-1 cost or revenue effect; ongoing effect; affected
funds; assumptions.]
Legal/Regulatory Considerations: [Statutes, charter provisions, prior
council action, contractual obligations.]
Implementation Timeline: [Key dates from adoption to operational.]

Option 2: [Title]
[Same structure.]

Option 3: [Title]
[Same structure.]

RECOMMENDATION
[Staff recommends Option [X] because [reason]. Caveats: [if any].]
[OR: Staff is presenting these options without a recommendation.]

REQUESTED COUNCIL ACTION
[Motion language council can read into the record.]

ATTACHMENTS
1. [Attachment]
2. [Attachment]
```

Length: typically 3 to 6 pages.

Tone: businesslike, plain language, neutral until the Recommendation section. Cite numbers — "$1.2M annually" reads better than "significant cost." Cite statutes — "under Tex. Loc. Gov't Code § 552.014" reads better than "as required by state law."

## TRAIGA & Compliance Notes

**AI use disclosure — transparency best practice (not a § 552.051 trigger):** Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 552.051(b)–(d) requires disclosure when a citizen *interacts directly with an AI system* (e.g., a chatbot, automated voice assistant, or AI-driven service interface). The disclosure trigger is *interaction*, not *output*. This skill produces a static document drafted with AI assistance; it does not put a citizen in direct interaction with AI, so § 552.051 is not engaged.

However, transparency about AI assistance in document drafting is sound practice and should align with the city's adopted AI use policy and the GovAI Coalition's guidance on documenting AI tool use. Consider a brief notation on documents distributed publicly or to the governing body: *"AI-assisted draft, reviewed by [staff title and name]."* This is a policy choice, not a statutory mandate.

**Public Records (Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 552):**

- A decision memo distributed in a public packet is a public record from the moment of distribution.
- A decision memo prepared specifically for executive session deliberation may be exempt under § 552.107 (attorney-client) if drafted by or with counsel, or § 552.111 (interagency/intra-agency memoranda) if it represents staff's pre-decisional advice. Routing matters: a memo emailed to council in the public packet is public; the same memo routed only to council under cover of a privileged executive session memo is treated differently.
- Do not include attorney-client privileged content (e.g., litigation strategy) in a memo intended for the public packet. If the analysis depends on privileged content, route the public memo and the privileged memo separately.
- Personal information of staff or third parties may need redaction under § 552.117, § 552.130, § 552.147 before public release.

**Ethics:** A decision memo authored by staff is staff work product, not the work of any individual council member. The memo should not advocate for political outcomes; it analyzes options and recommends based on professional judgment.

**Texas Open Meetings Act (Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 551):** The memo informs council deliberation but does not substitute for the posted agenda. The agenda controls the legal scope of action.

**Retention:** Decision memos that become part of the meeting record retain permanently with the minutes (Texas State Library Local Government Retention Schedule GR, Item 1000-25). Working drafts retain per the Administrative Records schedule.
